## Emerging Patterns (15 molecules, dataset complete)

*Observations from all 15 jurisdictions: NY, CA, US Federal, China, EU, UK, Singapore, South Korea, Australia, India, Indonesia, Germany, Canada, Netherlands, US State bills. Dataset complete. These are the paper's empirical findings.*

### 1. Institutional divergence is structural, not accidental

Every molecule shows at least one instrument where the institution actually relied on diverges from what the strategy theoretically implies. Fifteen for fifteen:
- **NY**: Disclosure law (A6765) relies on AG enforcement, not market discipline. Prohibition (S7882) recruits private litigants, not state command.
- **CA**: CCPA sweep deploys a rights-based statute as state enforcement tool, AG investigates, not consumers exercising opt-out.
- **China**: Every instrument operationally trusts the state, regardless of formal strategy. PIPL rights exist on paper; enforcement runs through CAC/SAMR/NDRC.
- **EU**: GDPR Art. 22 trusts individuals who don't activate for pricing. Competition enforcement (Art. 101/102) runs through the Commission, not market self-correction.
- **UK**: DMCCA disclosure provisions theoretically trust market discipline, but CMA deploys them through direct state enforcement (investigations, fines up to 10% turnover). DUAA 2025 weakens rights instrument, deliberate institutional choice to trust the market over individual rights.
- **Singapore**: Self-regulation theoretically trusts the market (voluntary adoption, industry-led governance). However, the state builds all the voluntary infrastructure, Model Framework, AI Verify, FEAT, Veritas, AIM Toolkit. State-orchestrated voluntary governance: the state designs the tools, industry decides whether to use them.
- **South Korea**: PIPA Art. 37-2 is a rights instrument (individuals challenge automated decisions), but the prior-informed-consent exception transforms it into a disclosure instrument in practice, the right to know why, not the right to say no. KFTC holds both competition and consumer protection mandates but deploys only the consumer protection channel (dark patterns) for pricing, not competition.
- **Australia**: ACL s.18 is formally a disclosure instrument (truthful representations required), but the ACCC and ASIC deploy it as state enforcement, identifying misleading pricing practices and prosecuting them (Webjet AUD 9M, IAG AUD 40M, Coles/Woolworths proceedings). Functionally closer to command and control than market-disciplining disclosure. ASIC insurance enforcement targets the same underlying conduct as UK FCA prohibition (price walking) but through a formally different strategy, disclosure-as-enforcement rather than sector-specific prohibition. The institution is equivalent; the strategy label differs.
- **US Federal**: Two divergences. (1) FTC 6(b) study is formally disclosure, but its production depends entirely on the political orientation of the Commission. When the institution changed (Ferguson replaced Khan), the disclosure instrument was neutralized. Disclosure here depends not on markets but on a politically contingent body choosing to produce information at all. (2) DOJ RealPage settlement is formally competition strategy, but the behavioral decree (software redesign, data-age restrictions, meeting conduct rules, monitor appointment) functions as de facto command-and-control regulation achieved through litigation rather than legislation.
- **India**: Two divergences. (1) DPDP Rules 2025 algorithmic impact assessments for SDFs are formally a rights-based instrument (protecting data principals from automated decisions), but operationally rely on state-designated SDF category, process compliance (conduct assessments, file reports), and Data Protection Board enforcement, not on individual data principals exercising rights. The DPDPA provides no individual right to challenge an algorithmic pricing decision. (2) CCI holds enforcement powers under the Competition Act (ss. 3 and 4) but its October 2025 market study recommends self-regulation (self-audits by firms) rather than enforcement, a deliberate institutional choice to defer, not a reflection of limited powers.
- **Indonesia**: UU 5/1999 Art. 6 makes price discrimination per se illegal, a competition instrument with the strongest possible formal prohibition. However, KPPU has never applied it to algorithmic pricing or any digital market context. The instrument theoretically relies on KPPU enforcement; in practice, KPPU lacks institutional capacity (no search and seizure authority until the pending amendment) and has not treated Art. 6 as reaching consumer-facing algorithmic pricing. The formally strongest prohibition in the dataset is operationally dormant for this problem.

- **Germany**: Section 312d BGB personalized pricing disclosure (Omnibus Directive transposition) is formally a disclosure instrument trusting market discipline. However, enforcement depends on decentralized state authorities with a EUR 25,000 maximum fine, trivial for major retailers. The Bundeskartellamt, a competition authority, is the actual enforcement actor for algorithmic pricing (Amazon EUR 59M disgorgement), not the consumer protection apparatus. The operative institution for pricing enforcement is competition, not the disclosure channel that formally addresses it.
- **Canada**: The institution with the strongest enforcement powers (Competition Bureau, AMPs up to 3% global turnover, Competition Tribunal adjudication) structurally cannot reach unilateral personalized pricing (requires agreement or dominance). The institution with the most relevant legal principles for the data infrastructure (OPC, consent, purpose limitation) cannot levy penalties or issue binding orders. Quebec's CAI has both relevant principles (Law 25 ADM) and strong penalties (4% global turnover) but only provincial jurisdiction. The enforcement gap is distributed across three institutions, each holding a piece of the puzzle.
- **Netherlands**: ACM's combined competition + consumer protection mandate theoretically eliminates the institutional fragmentation that plagues other jurisdictions, a single authority can use whichever legal basis is most effective. However, in practice, the ACM has chosen market investigation (aviation sector, 2025) and soft-law guidelines rather than enforcement against algorithmic pricing. The AFM's insurance work (finding almost half of non-life insurers charge loyal customers higher margins) uses supervisory guidance rather than enforcement orders. The most institutionally unified authority in the dataset operates through the softest instruments.
- **US State bills**: The legislative wave (51+ bills across 24+ states) formally proposes prohibition as the dominant strategy, but the operative institution is industry opposition, the CalChamber, TechNet, and NRF have effectively determined which bills survive (disclosure, grocery-specific) and which die (general prohibition). The political economy institution, not the legislative one, is the operative gatekeeper. NRF even attempted to use the judiciary (First Amendment challenge to NY A6765) as a veto institution, the challenge failed, but the attempt illustrates that industry treats multiple institutions as enforcement channels for its preferred outcome.

**Implication**: The divergence between formal strategy and operational institution may be the most consistent cross-jurisdictional pattern. Fifteen for fifteen, this is the paper's central empirical finding.

### 2. Prohibition is hard, and directionally constrained

- **China**: Only jurisdiction with prohibition as primary strategy. Achieved through unified state direction over seven years.
- **NY**: Achieved sector-specific prohibition (rental housing only). Required a salient harm story (tenants, not consumers in general).
- **CA**: Tried prohibition three times (AB 446, SB 259, SB 295). All failed. Political economy draws a line: coordination targetable, personalization not.
- **EU**: Never attempted prohibition. Parliament study floated it (2022); no legislative follow-up.
- **UK**: Achieved sector-specific prohibition (insurance only, FCA price walking ban 2022). Required a sector regulator with dedicated market study, quantified harm (GBP 1.2B overcharge, 6M affected policyholders), and institutional authority to act without legislation. But: 40% motor insurance price increase post-ban, prohibition achieved its objective but produced market-wide price adjustment.
- **US Federal**: H.R. 4640 is the first federal prohibition proposal (bans AI-based individualized pricing). Near-zero prospect of passage, introduced by progressive Democrats, no Republican co-sponsors, hostile congressional environment. The only federal proposal that reaches unilateral personalized pricing. S. 232 codifies the competition approach (algorithmic collusion) but deliberately avoids prohibition of personalized pricing itself.

- **India**: No prohibition attempted or achieved. Draft Digital Competition Bill 2024 proposes ex ante structural obligations but not prohibition of personalized pricing, and remains unintroduced. The CCI chose guidance over enforcement despite holding enforcement powers. India's Art. 6 equivalent does not exist, no per se price discrimination prohibition in consumer-facing markets.
- **Indonesia**: UU 5/1999 Art. 6 is a per se illegal price discrimination provision, formally the strongest prohibition in the dataset. However, it was designed for explicit B2B agreements, KPPU has never applied it to algorithmic or consumer-facing pricing, and KPPU lacks the institutional capacity to enforce it in digital markets. A prohibition that exists on paper but has never been activated confirms the pattern from a different angle: even where prohibition is formally available, it remains dormant without institutional capacity and political will.

**Implication**: Prohibition requires either concentrated state capacity (China), a sector-specific harm narrative (NY rental), or a sector regulator with quantified evidence and institutional independence (UK insurance). General prohibition of personalized pricing faces political economy resistance everywhere else. Indonesia adds a new dimension: formal prohibition (Art. 6 per se illegality) without institutional activation, the statute exists but the enforcement body cannot or does not deploy it. The US Federal level remains the sharpest data point: even in the jurisdiction with the richest evidence base ($3.8B quantified harm, 250+ retailers identified, landmark antitrust settlement), general prohibition cannot advance through Congress. The political economy boundary is the same as California, coordination is targetable, personalization is not.

### 3. Three distinct design logics confirmed

| Logic | Jurisdiction | Signature |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| **Unified** | China, Singapore | China: one direction, each instrument tightens. Escalation sequence from disclosure to prohibition (2019–2026). Singapore: one direction, but toward voluntarism, each instrument adds governance infrastructure without binding obligation. Same coherence, opposite vector. |
| **Differentiated** | NY, UK | Different strategies for different sectors. NY: same legislative moment, deliberate pluralism. UK: different regulators acting in different eras under different mandates, institutional differentiation. |
| **Accumulated** | CA, EU, South Korea, Australia, US Federal, India, Indonesia, Germany, Canada, Netherlands, US States | Different instruments from different eras/actors/legal bases. No coordination. |

CA, EU, South Korea, Australia, US Federal, India, Indonesia, Germany, Canada, Netherlands, and US States are all accumulated but differ in mechanism: CA's accumulation was forced by political economy (prohibition blocked, AG pivoted laterally); EU's accumulation is structural (instruments from different Treaty bases, different policy eras, different enforcement institutions); South Korea's accumulation is responsive (each instrument responds to a different international trend, GDPR influence on PIPA, dark pattern movement on E-Commerce Act, global AI governance wave on AI Basic Act, producing dense but uncoordinated coverage); Australia's accumulation follows a study-to-legislation pipeline (DPSI five-year inquiry → recommendations → exposure draft bill), giving accumulated instruments a sequential logic even though they were not designed as a coordinated response to surveillance pricing; US Federal's accumulation is enforcement-driven and compounded by partisan discontinuity, DOJ prosecutes collusion under antitrust law, FTC studied the practice under consumer protection, Congress proposed legislation under different theories, the White House issued and revoked an executive order, and the 2025 administration change severed the consumer protection track while preserving the antitrust track. India's accumulation is enforcement-triggered, regulators respond to consumer complaints (CCPA Uber/Ola) and global trends (CCI market study paralleling UK CMA) rather than legislating; the most advanced proposed instrument (Digital Competition Bill 2024) remains unintroduced. Indonesia's accumulation reflects institutional fragmentation and legislative lag, instruments from 1999 (consumer protection, competition), 2019 (ride-hailing tariff caps, e-commerce), 2022 (PDP Law), and 2023-2025 (OJK AI guidelines, Permendag 31/2023) were designed for entirely different problems, and the competition law is being amended to reach digital markets two decades after enactment. Germany's accumulation has a competition center of gravity, the Bundeskartellamt drives both intellectual leadership (joint studies, sector inquiries) and enforcement (Amazon, Meta, Google), while consumer protection and data protection instruments arrived as EU transposition without domestic initiative. The GWB amendments (10th in 2021, 11th in 2023) progressively expanded the competition toolkit, giving the accumulation a discernible direction even though the instruments as a set were not designed for surveillance pricing. Canada's accumulation is competition-dominated but structurally gapped, the 2022 and 2024 Competition Act amendments significantly strengthened drip pricing, abuse of dominance, and competitor collaboration tools, but privacy law (PIPEDA) stalled (Bill C-27 died), AI governance (AIDA) died, and provincial ADM law (Quebec Law 25) applies only in one province. The accumulation has a federal-provincial dimension absent elsewhere in the dataset.

NY and UK are both differentiated but differ in mechanism: NY's differentiation is legislative (one legislature choosing different strategies for different sectors in the same session); UK's differentiation is institutional (different sector regulators, FCA, CMA, ICO, acting independently under different mandates in different eras). This suggests differentiated design logic has two subtypes: legislative and institutional.

China and Singapore are both unified but in opposite directions: China unified around escalating prohibition; Singapore unified around expanding voluntarism. Same coherence of design, opposite regulatory vectors. This confirms unified design logic is about institutional coordination, not regulatory intensity, a jurisdiction can be unified whether it commands or convenes.

**Question for remaining molecules**: Does any jurisdiction exhibit a fourth design logic? Or do all map onto unified/differentiated/accumulated?

### 4. Chien's one-way escalation holds only for China

- **China**: One-directional escalation, disclosure (2019) → rights (2021) → code-as-regulation (2022) → prohibition (2025). Classic escalation.
- **NY**: Not escalation, parallel strategies deployed simultaneously. No temporal sequence.
- **CA**: Attempted escalation, blocked. Then lateral pivot (antitrust → privacy). Movement is constrained, not one-way.
- **EU**: Accumulation without direction. Each layer adds a strategy but doesn't escalate beyond the previous.

**Implication**: One-way escalation may be a feature of unified design logic only. Accumulated and differentiated jurisdictions move laterally, get blocked, or add layers without escalating. The "regulatory ladder" metaphor is wrong for most of the dataset, the topology metaphor (positions on a map, not steps on a ladder) is validated by the data.

### 5. The proprietary pricing gap

Every jurisdiction struggles to reach unilateral, single-firm personalized pricing:
- **NY**: A6765 disclosure touches it. S7882 prohibition is sector-specific only.
- **CA**: AB 325 explicitly doesn't reach proprietary algorithms. CCPA sweep is the untested workaround.
- **China**: Price Behavior Rules reach it (direct prohibition), but enforcement hasn't started.
- **EU**: CRD Art. 6(1)(ea) requires disclosure only. GDPR Art. 22 is contested. Competition law requires dominance or coordination.
- **UK**: FCA ban reaches proprietary pricing algorithms but only in insurance. DMCCA targets drip pricing, not personalization. DUAA 2025 actively facilitates algorithmic pricing by loosening ADM restrictions. CMA dynamic pricing project explicitly distinguished personalized pricing and chose not to address it.
- **Singapore**: No instrument reaches proprietary personalized pricing. PDPA consent framework could theoretically constrain it (purpose limitation), but organizations draft purposes broadly. No automated decision-making right. The most sophisticated voluntary governance infrastructure in the dataset, and it doesn't touch pricing.
- **South Korea**: PIPA Art. 37-2 could theoretically reach it (automated decision-making right), but prior-informed-consent exception neutralizes the refusal right. KFTC enforces against algorithm manipulation (self-preferencing) but not consumer-facing pricing. AI Basic Act could reach it if Presidential Decrees designate pricing as high-impact, but this is uncertain. Densest instrument set in APAC, and still can't touch proprietary personalized pricing.
- **US Federal**: The sharpest illustration. DOJ RealPage reaches algorithmic collusion (shared data) but explicitly not unilateral pricing, Duane Morris: "DOJ is not treating algorithmic pricing as inherently illegal." FTC v. Amazon targets algorithmic price leadership (Project Nessie) but not personalized pricing. S. 232 codifies the collusion theory. Only H.R. 4640 reaches proprietary personalized pricing, and it has near-zero prospect of passage. The FTC 6(b) study found 250+ retailers using surveillance pricing, quantifying the practice's scale, but the study creates no enforcement authority. The richest evidence base in the dataset, and the proprietary gap is wider than anywhere else.
- **India**: CCPA Uber/Ola investigation targets observable device-based differential pricing (iPhone vs. Android), the most visible manifestation, not the deeper behavioral profiling. No instrument reaches proprietary algorithmic pricing based on browsing history, willingness-to-pay modeling, or behavioral data. E-Commerce Rules 2020 Rule 4 prohibits "arbitrary classification" but has never been tested on algorithmic segmentation. DPDPA provides no right to challenge automated pricing decisions. CCI dismissed surge pricing complaints without examining pricing methodology. Closest instrument: DPDP Rules 2025 Rule 13(3) algorithmic impact assessments, but process obligation for SDFs only, not a substantive constraint on pricing.
- **Indonesia**: No instrument reaches proprietary personalized pricing. UU 5/1999 Art. 6 (per se illegal price discrimination) was designed for B2B agreements and has never been applied to consumer-facing algorithmic pricing. PDP Law Art. 10 right to object to automated decisions is untested and weaker than GDPR Art. 22 (right to object vs. right not to be subject to). Ride-hailing tariff caps constrain algorithmic pricing within bands but do not address personalization within the band, platforms retain discretion to charge different passengers different amounts so long as both prices fall within the floor and ceiling.
- **Germany**: The Bundeskartellamt's Amazon case (EUR 59M disgorgement) targets algorithmic pricing mechanisms, but platform-to-seller price control, not consumer-facing personalized pricing. Section 19a reaches designated firms only. Section 312d BGB requires disclosure of personalized pricing but has produced zero enforcement actions in nearly four years. The DEK explicitly classified personalized pricing as Level 3 risk requiring approval with regular controls, but the EU AI Act that its framework influenced classified general algorithmic pricing as minimal risk. The Verbraucherzentrale's 2018 finding (dynamic but rarely individualized pricing in German e-commerce) has not been updated. Most developed enforcement infrastructure in the EU for algorithmic pricing mechanisms, and it still can't reach proprietary consumer-facing personalization.
- **Canada**: Competition law (s. 45, ss. 78-79, s. 90.1) requires either an agreement or dominance, a single non-dominant firm unilaterally deploying personalized pricing falls outside all instruments. PIPEDA constrains data collection for pricing but has no ADM provisions and weak enforcement (OPC cannot levy penalties). Quebec Law 25 s. 12.1 is the closest Canadian instrument, requires transparency, explanation, and human review for automated decisions, but provincial scope only and untested for pricing. The Bureau's discussion paper explicitly identified the gap between coordination (reachable) and unilateral personalization (unreachable). Private right of action (eff. June 2025) could enable class action-adjacent litigation but is untested.

**Implication**: The shared algorithm / coordination problem is easy to regulate (antitrust frame, bipartisan/cross-jurisdiction legitimacy). The proprietary personalization problem is hard to regulate. This may be the empirical signature of the one-way mirror through-line candidate, the algorithm sees the consumer, but the regulatory instrument can't see the algorithm when it's proprietary and unilateral. US Federal is the clearest proof: even with landmark enforcement ($3.8B quantified harm, consent decrees, trial pending), the gap between coordination and personalization is unbridgeable through existing federal law.

### 6. Direct action appears, but only through political economy, not regulatory theory

Indonesia's ride-hailing tariff cap (Permenhub 12/2019) is the first direct-action strategy in the dataset, the state sets price bands and platforms must operate within them. No other jurisdiction uses Baldwin's direct-action strategy for algorithmic pricing. However, the instrument was not designed as surveillance pricing regulation: it was driven by driver protests, consumer complaints about surge pricing, and competition between traditional taxis and app-based platforms. The state intervened because the political cost of unchecked surge pricing became unacceptable, not because it diagnosed an information asymmetry or market failure in algorithmic pricing.

**Implication**: Direct action on algorithmic pricing exists in the dataset, but only where political economy forced it, and only in a single sector (transportation). This may explain its absence elsewhere: direct action requires the political salience of visible, widespread consumer harm. Personalized pricing based on behavioral profiling lacks the visibility that triggered Indonesia's intervention (everyone could see different surge prices). The same one-way mirror dynamic that makes proprietary pricing hard to regulate (Pattern 5) also explains why direct action hasn't emerged as a response.

### 7. Failed instruments are data

CA's three failed prohibition bills, the EU Parliament's unfollowed 2022 study, and the US Federal landscape are as informative as enacted instruments. They reveal the political economy boundary: where each jurisdiction draws the line between acceptable intervention and industry resistance. US Federal adds two new categories: the revoked instrument (EO 14110, AI governance reversed by administration change) and the paused instrument (FTC 6(b) study, effectively suspended by new FTC Chair). Both reveal that federal instruments are vulnerable to partisan discontinuity in ways that state-level instruments are not. India adds a stalled instrument: the Draft Digital Competition Bill 2024, which completed public consultation but has not been introduced in Parliament, stalled not by political opposition (as in CA) but by inter-ministerial coordination delay. Indonesia adds multiple pending instruments simultaneously: competition law amendment, presidential regulation on AI, and consumer protection law overhaul, all in active deliberation but none enacted, suggesting a jurisdiction where the awareness of the gap exists but the legislative machinery moves slowly. Including failed, revoked, paused, stalled, and pending instruments in the dataset strengthens the empirical contribution.

## Initial Assessment (15/15 complete)

*Dataset complete. Assessment before alloy.*

### What the data holds

- **Institutional divergence is the paper's central empirical finding.** 15/15, every jurisdiction, no exception. Baldwin's strategy taxonomy describes what regulation *intends* to do; institutional divergence describes what it *actually* does. The gap between formal strategy and operative institution is universal. This is a contribution to [[baldwin-regulatory-strategies]], not just an application.
- **The proprietary pricing gap is the empirical signature of the [[algorithmic-agnosticism-wtp|agnosticism pit]].** 15/15, no jurisdiction can reach unilateral, single-firm personalized pricing based on behavioral data. The coordination/collusion problem is tractable (antitrust frame: [[sp-us-federal|DOJ RealPage]], [[sp-south-korea|KFTC Coupang]], [[sp-germany|Bundeskartellamt Amazon]]). The personalization problem is not. WTP bundles preference and bias; the regulatory instrument can't see inside the proprietary algorithm when it's unilateral. The one-way mirror, confirmed empirically across 15 jurisdictions.
- **The [[regulatory-topology-not-ladder|topology metaphor]] is validated.** Chien's one-way escalation holds only for [[sp-china|China]]. Every other jurisdiction moves laterally, gets blocked, accumulates, or reverses. Strategies coexist as positions on a map, not steps on a sequence.
- **Political economy constrains the topology.** The US States molecule draws the sharpest line: disclosure passes, sector-specific grocery prohibition passes, general prohibition does not pass (100% failure rate). Industry opposition (CalChamber, TechNet, NRF) shapes which strategies survive. The "choice" among Baldwin's strategies isn't free when some are structurally blocked. Connect to [[collective-action-problem|Olson's logic]], diffuse consumer harm vs. concentrated industry opposition.
- **The GDPR Art. 22 gradient.** The automated decision-making right, theoretically the most direct instrument for reaching algorithmic pricing, fails everywhere it exists. EU: contested, unenforced. UK: being weakened by DUAA 2025. South Korea: neutralized by consent exception. Indonesia: weaker formulation. India: deliberately excluded. Singapore, US, Australia: absent or weakest possible. The rights-based approach is a theoretical solution that doesn't work in practice for pricing. May deserve its own section in the alloy.
- **Direct action exists but only through political economy, not regulatory theory.** Indonesia's ride-hailing tariff cap is the sole instance in 15 jurisdictions. Driven by political salience of visible surge pricing (everyone sees different prices). Personalized pricing based on behavioral profiling lacks this visibility, the same opacity that creates the proprietary gap also prevents the political mobilization needed for direct action.

### What's uncertain

- **Is "accumulated" a finding or a tautology?** 11/15 jurisdictions accumulated. If the taxonomy is this skewed, maybe the finding is: only two jurisdictions in the world ([[sp-china|China]] and [[sp-singapore|Singapore]]) have *designed* a response to surveillance pricing. Everyone else is improvising. That reframes "accumulated" not as a design logic but as the *absence* of design logic. The subtypes (enforcement-triggered, study-to-legislation, partisan discontinuity, competition center of gravity, institutionally coordinated) become the real analytical payload. **Question: does the alloy treat accumulated as a third design logic, or as the default condition from which unified and differentiated are departures?**
- **Web-searched evidence needs spot-checking.** The molecules were populated through agent web search. Specific factual claims, [[sp-germany|EUR 59M Amazon disgorgement]], [[sp-us-states|NM HB 285 passing 67-0/40-0]], [[sp-netherlands|AFM finding half of non-life insurers charge loyal customers higher margins]], [[sp-canada|Cineplex CAD 38.9M]], should be verified against primary sources before the alloy relies on them. Structure is sound; details need verification at the instrument level.
- **Whether the EU overlay jurisdictions (Germany, Netherlands) add enough.** Both are accumulated with competition focus. Germany: adversarial enforcement (disgorgement). Netherlands: soft guidance and investigation. The contrast is interesting (regulatory culture) but may not justify full separate treatment in the alloy vs. being folded into the EU section with national variation notes. **Question: does the alloy give Germany and Netherlands their own sections, or treat them as EU overlays?**

### What surprised

- **US States reframes the political economy story.** The failure rate IS the finding. The wave is accelerating (5x growth 2024→2025, continued into 2026) but passage isn't. Industry opposition isn't just blocking bills, it's shaping the topology itself. The viable strategies are constrained by political economy. The grocery sector is viable because of necessity framing (bread, milk, produce neutralize the "innovation/personalization" argument). The ESL moratorium thread ([[sp-us-states|WA HB 2481]], MD SB 387) is a [[code-as-regulation]]-adjacent strategy: regulating the infrastructure rather than the pricing practice.
- **The Indonesia-Singapore pair is a natural experiment.** Same ASEAN region, same regulatory gap, completely different institutional reasons. Singapore: deliberate absence backed by the most sophisticated voluntary governance infrastructure in the dataset. Indonesia: fragmented absence with one politically-driven sector-specific intervention nobody else has. "Absence" is not one thing.
- **The Germany-Netherlands pair reveals regulatory culture.** Same EU baseline, same accumulated logic, same competition focus. Germany: Bundeskartellamt wields EUR 59M disgorgement orders. Netherlands: ACM launches soft-law investigations and guidelines. Both institutionally advanced; entirely different enforcement temperaments. **Question: is regulatory culture a variable the paper should name?**

### What we haven't thought about

*From dwelling session, Feb 26 2026. Released judgment. High-level view.*

- **The empty positions on the topology.** Two of Baldwin's nine strategies appear zero times across 15 jurisdictions: **incentive-based** and **nudge**. Direct action appears exactly once ([[sp-indonesia|Indonesia]]). No jurisdiction has tried tax incentives for transparent pricing. No jurisdiction has designed choice architecture to help consumers navigate personalized pricing. No jurisdiction uses subsidies or rewards for non-discriminatory algorithms. The topology has blank spaces. Is it because these strategies are unsuited to the problem, or because no one has thought to try them? The absence of strategies may be as informative as the presence of strategies.
- **Almost nothing was designed for this problem.** Count the instruments actually *designed* for surveillance pricing: China's Price Behavior Rules, NY A6765, CRD Omnibus amendment (Art. 6(1)(ea)), the US State bills. Maybe five or six out of ~150. Everything else, competition law, consumer protection, data protection, AI governance, was designed for a different problem and is being *stretched* toward this one. If the majority of instruments are repurposed, then "accumulated" isn't just "no coordination", it's "no design at all." The instruments weren't built for the object they're being asked to regulate. This may explain why institutional divergence is universal: the institution that designed the instrument had a different problem in mind.
- **The regulatory object is remarkably recent.** "Surveillance pricing" as a named thing barely existed before the FTC's 2024 6(b) study framing. The instruments preceded the object, they were there for other reasons and are now being redirected. We're documenting the birth of a regulatory category, not mapping a mature regulatory landscape.
- **The enforcement paradox.** Jurisdictions with the *most* instruments tend to have the *least* enforcement on surveillance pricing specifically. South Korea: densest APAC set, zero enforcement on personalized pricing. EU: eight instruments, no enforcement under CRD or GDPR for pricing. Germany: zero personalized pricing disclosure enforcement in four years. The enforcement that exists is always *near* but never *on* the thing: drip pricing ([[sp-canada|Canada]]), algorithm manipulation ([[sp-south-korea|South Korea]]), data exploitation ([[sp-germany|Germany Meta]]), platform seller pricing ([[sp-germany|Germany Amazon]]), misleading discounts ([[sp-netherlands|Netherlands]]). Different from the proprietary pricing gap (what instruments *can't* reach), this is about what institutions *choose not to* reach, or can't detect, or can't prove.
- **The consumer as absent subject.** In all 15 jurisdictions, the regulatory conversation is between institutions, industry, and advocacy organizations. The person whose data is used to set their price is almost never the active agent. Rights-based instruments give consumers rights they don't exercise for pricing. The only complaint-driven enforcement ([[sp-india|India Uber/Ola]]) happened because the difference was *visible* (iPhone vs. Android screenshots going viral). Where personalization is invisible, the consumer can't complain, can't exercise rights, can't mobilize politically. The one-way mirror is not just a regulatory problem, it's a democratic problem. The subject who would need to activate the instrument can't see the thing they'd need to activate it against. Connects to [[algorithmic-agnosticism-wtp]] at a deeper level than the pricing mechanism, it's about the *conditions of possibility* for regulatory activation.
- **Who's not in the room.** No Japan, no Brazil (LGPD has ADM provisions), no Israel, no GCC, no African Union. Dataset weighted toward North America + EU + APAC + China. Selection based on available intel, not systematic sampling. The paper should name this boundary.

### The definitional problem, what is the beast?

*We need to establish the variation of how jurisdictions define or capture the phenomenon. Is "surveillance pricing" a genus, a species, or a different beast entirely?*

The dataset reveals that jurisdictions don't agree on what they're regulating. The same practice gets captured under different names, different legal categories, and different conceptual frames:

- **"Surveillance pricing"**, the FTC's 2024 framing. Used by US State bills. Foregrounds the *data collection* (surveillance) feeding into the *pricing decision*. Implies a consumer harm frame.
- **"Personalized pricing"**, the EU/UK frame (CRD Art. 6(1)(ea), Omnibus Directive). Foregrounds the *individualization*. More neutral, personalization can be beneficial or harmful. The disclosure obligation uses this term.
- **"Dynamic pricing"**, often conflated with personalized pricing but technically different. Dynamic pricing responds to *market conditions* (supply, demand, time). Personalized pricing responds to *individual data*. The Bundeskartellamt/Autorite de la concurrence 2019 joint study distinguished them carefully. Most jurisdictions don't. The [[sp-canada|Competition Bureau's June 2025 discussion paper]] explicitly distinguished the two, one of the few regulatory documents to do so.
- **"Algorithmic pricing"**, the broadest frame. Includes dynamic, personalized, and coordinated pricing by algorithms. Competition authorities (CCI, KFTC, Bundeskartellamt) tend to use this term because it captures both collusion and personalization under one umbrella.
- **"Price discrimination"**, the traditional economic/legal frame. [[sp-indonesia|UU 5/1999 Art. 6]] uses this. Implies B2B price discrimination (Robinson-Patman tradition), not B2C behavioral profiling. The legal concept doesn't map onto the technological practice.
- **"Dashuju shashu" (大数据杀熟)**, China's frame. "Big data kills familiarity", the practice of charging loyal/returning customers higher prices. Foregrounds the *betrayal of trust* by the platform that knows you. Culturally specific; captures something the English-language terms miss.
- **"Price walking"**, the UK FCA's frame for insurance. Loyalty penalty, raising prices for renewing customers. Sector-specific term for a practice that overlaps with but isn't identical to personalized pricing.
- **"Drip pricing"**, Canada, UK (DMCCA), US States. Gradually adding fees during checkout. Adjacent to but distinct from personalized pricing, targets price disaggregation, not individualization.

**Question for the alloy: does the paper need a definitional section?** If jurisdictions don't agree on what the beast is, then the comparative exercise is comparing instruments aimed at *different conceptions of the same practice*. The variation in naming is not just terminological, it reflects different understandings of what the harm is:
- Surveillance pricing: the harm is the *data collection*
- Personalized pricing: the harm is the *price individualization*
- Algorithmic pricing: the harm is the *opacity of the mechanism*
- Price discrimination: the harm is the *unfairness of different prices*
- Dashuju shashu: the harm is the *betrayal of the relationship*
- Price walking: the harm is the *exploitation of loyalty*
- Drip pricing: the harm is the *deception in presentation*

These are not the same harm. They overlap but don't coincide. A paper that maps regulatory instruments across jurisdictions needs to establish what it's mapping instruments *against*. The genus/species question: is "surveillance pricing" the genus that contains all of these? Or is it one species alongside others? Or is the real genus something unnamed, the use of *information asymmetry created by digital infrastructure* to extract consumer surplus, with all the above as species?

**This may need to be Section I of the alloy.** Before the topology, before Baldwin, before the comparative exercise: what is the regulatory object? The answer isn't a definition, it's a map of how the object is differently constituted across jurisdictions, and an argument for what unifies them.

### Inconspicuousness as the structural condition

The dataset suggests that the central regulatory challenge of surveillance pricing is not its legality, its harmfulness, or its novelty, but its *inconspicuousness*. The personalized price presents no phenomenal signature, it appears to the consumer as an ordinary price. Unlike drip pricing (where fees are visibly added), surge pricing (where the price spike is observable to all users simultaneously), or price walking (where the renewal premium is higher than the acquisition premium), unilateral personalized pricing based on behavioral profiling produces an output, a price, that is indistinguishable from any other price. The consumer has no counterfactual: no knowledge of what another consumer paid, no basis for comparison, no visible marker that the price was individualized.

This inconspicuousness has structural implications for the regulatory topology. Each of [[baldwin-regulatory-strategies|Baldwin's]] strategies presupposes a different degree of visibility in the practice it addresses:

- **Command and control (prohibition)** presupposes that the practice can be defined with sufficient precision to prohibit. Where the practice is inconspicuous, definitional precision is difficult. Prohibition has been achieved only where state capacity can render the practice visible through regulatory command ([[sp-china|China]]) or where the species is already conspicuous: insurance price walking ([[sp-uk|UK FCA]]), rental housing ([[sp-new-york|NY S7882]]), grocery shelf pricing ([[sp-us-states|NM HB 285]]).
- **Competition** presupposes evidence of coordination, shared data sources, common algorithmic intermediaries, hub-and-spoke structures. Algorithmic collusion produces evidentiary traces (shared inputs, parallel outputs). Unilateral personalized pricing does not. This explains why competition enforcement consistently reaches the collusion species ([[sp-us-federal|DOJ RealPage]], [[sp-south-korea|KFTC Coupang]], [[sp-germany|Bundeskartellamt Amazon]]) but not the personalization species.
- **Disclosure** presupposes that the obligation to disclose will be enforced and that the disclosed information will be actionable. The Omnibus Directive's transposition (CRD Art. 6(1)(ea)) created a disclosure obligation across the EU, yet no enforcement action has been brought in any member state in nearly four years ([[sp-germany|Germany]], [[sp-netherlands|Netherlands]]). The inconspicuousness of the practice may explain the enforcement gap: if neither regulators nor consumers can readily identify when personalized pricing occurs, the disclosure obligation lacks a detection mechanism.
- **Rights and liabilities** presupposes that the rights-holder knows the practice is occurring. The GDPR Art. 22 right not to be subject to automated decision-making presupposes a data subject who is aware that an automated decision has been made. If the personalized price is indistinguishable from an ordinary price, the precondition for exercising the right is absent. This may explain the GDPR Art. 22 gradient observed across the dataset: the right exists in multiple jurisdictions but is not exercised for pricing in any of them.
- **Direct action** presupposes political salience sufficient to justify state intervention in market pricing. Political salience requires visible harm. [[sp-indonesia|Indonesia's]] ride-hailing tariff cap emerged because surge pricing was conspicuous, different passengers could observe different prices at the same time, and driver associations could organize around the visible disparity. Behavioral personalization lacks this conspicuousness.
- **Incentive-based** and **nudge** strategies are entirely absent from the dataset (zero instances across 15 jurisdictions). Both would require addressing the inconspicuousness directly: incentivizing firms to reveal a practice they benefit from concealing, or designing choice architecture for consumers who do not know they are subject to differential pricing. The absence of these strategies from the dataset may reflect the structural difficulty of designing regulatory interventions for a practice that resists detection.

The topology thus maps not only the distribution of regulatory strategies across jurisdictions, but the *visibility requirements* of those strategies against a practice whose defining structural feature is inconspicuousness. Where the practice produces observable traces (coordination, drip pricing, surge pricing, loyalty penalties), instruments cluster and enforcement occurs. Where the practice is inconspicuous (unilateral behavioral personalization), the topology is sparse and enforcement is absent. The proprietary pricing gap identified across all 15 jurisdictions may be understood as a consequence of this structural condition: the inconspicuousness of the core practice defeats the visibility requirements of the available regulatory strategies.

This framing connects the comparative regulatory findings to the [[algorithmic-agnosticism-wtp|agnosticism problem]] identified in the surveillance pricing literature. The agnosticism critique holds that willingness-to-pay estimates bundle consumer preference and structural vulnerability in ways that the pricing algorithm cannot and does not distinguish. The inconspicuousness analysis extends this: the regulatory instrument, like the consumer, cannot distinguish a personalized price from an ordinary price. The information asymmetry that the algorithm exploits (the firm knows the consumer's data; the consumer does not know the firm's algorithm) is reproduced at the regulatory level (the firm knows the pricing methodology; the regulator cannot observe it in operation). The one-way mirror operates in both directions, toward the consumer and toward the state.

### Abductive structure for the alloy

The empirical findings support the following abductive structure:

1. **The regulatory object**, The practice of using personal data to set individualized prices constitutes a family of related but distinct practices, differently named and differently constituted across jurisdictions. The variation in naming (surveillance pricing, personalized pricing, algorithmic pricing, price discrimination, dashuju shashu, price walking, drip pricing) reflects different conceptions of the underlying harm, each shaped by the instruments available. The core species, unilateral personalized pricing based on behavioral data, is distinguished from adjacent species by its inconspicuousness.
2. **The regulatory topology**, Mapping [[baldwin-regulatory-strategies|Baldwin's]] nine strategies across 15 jurisdictions reveals where instruments cluster (around conspicuous species: collusion, drip pricing, surge pricing, loyalty penalties) and where the topology is empty (around the inconspicuous core species, and in two strategy categories, incentive-based and nudge, that would require addressing inconspicuousness directly). The topology describes positions on a map ([[regulatory-topology-not-ladder]]), not steps on a ladder: one-way escalation holds only for [[sp-china|China]].
3. **The empirical findings**, (a) Institutional divergence between formal strategy and operative institution: 15/15 jurisdictions. (b) No jurisdiction can effectively regulate unilateral personalized pricing based on behavioral data: 15/15. (c) Accumulated (undesigned) regulatory architecture is the default: 11/15. (d) Enforcement occurs on adjacent, conspicuous species but not on the inconspicuous core species (the enforcement paradox). (e) The automated decision-making right (GDPR Art. 22 and equivalents) is inoperative for pricing in every jurisdiction where it exists.
4. **The argument**, Regulatory instruments presuppose varying degrees of visibility in the practices they address. The defining structural feature of unilateral personalized pricing is its inconspicuousness, it produces no phenomenal signature distinguishable from ordinary pricing. This mismatch between the visibility requirements of existing regulatory strategies and the inconspicuousness of the core practice is structural, not accidental, and explains why approximately 150 instruments across 15 jurisdictions have not produced effective regulation of the practice they nominally address. The instruments regulate what is visible; the core practice is not.
