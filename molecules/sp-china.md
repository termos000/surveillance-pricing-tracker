# China — Surveillance Pricing Regulatory Profile

*Jurisdiction molecule for [[surveillance-pricing-comparative-regulation|Paper 1]].*

China deploys the most comprehensive instrument set in the global landscape — prohibition as primary strategy, supported by data protection rights, disclosure, competition enforcement, and algorithm governance — unified under a single policy direction of state-led consumer protection, with rights-based instruments that formally trust individuals but operationally trust the state.

## Instruments

| PIPL Article 24 (eff. Nov 1, 2021) | Rights & liabilities | General | Operative | CAC primary; SAMR, sectoral regulators; fines up to CNY 50M or 5% of prior year revenue |

| Internet Information Service Algorithmic Recommendation Management Provisions (eff. March 1, 2022) | Code-as-regulation | General (internet information services) | Operative | CAC (coordination), SAMR, MIIT, Ministry of Public Security; fines up to CNY 100,000; algorithm filing requirement |

## Detail

### Rules for Price Behavior on Internet Platforms (Dec 2025, eff. April 10, 2026)

The National Development and Reform Commission (NDRC), State Administration for Market Regulation (SAMR), and Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC) jointly published these rules on December 9, 2025, as Development and Reform Price Regulation [2025] No. 1607. The rules contain seven chapters and 29 articles, establishing a normalized supervision framework with a five-year validity period through April 10, 2031.

**Core prohibition — Article 15.** Prohibits platform operators from setting different prices or charging standards for the same goods or services under the same transaction conditions without consumers' knowledge, based on information such as willingness to pay, ability to pay, consumption preferences, and consumption habits, using data, algorithms, platform rules, or other means. This provision directly targets the practice known in China as "big data backstabbing" (dashuju shashu, 大数据杀熟) — the use of algorithms to charge loyal or high-spending customers higher prices for identical goods.

**Merchant pricing autonomy.** Platforms must not intervene in merchant pricing through traffic restrictions, search ranking demotions, or algorithm penalties. Platforms are prohibited from leveraging dominant market position to impose "lowest price" agreements — a direct response to predatory price-cutting practices among e-commerce platforms.

**Transparency requirements.** Platforms and merchants must clearly label prices and prominently display rules for dynamic pricing. Promotional activities, subsidies, differential pricing, and ranking practices must be transparently disclosed. Fee changes to merchants require a solicitation period of no less than seven days.

**Record-keeping obligations — Article 24.** Platforms must properly preserve relevant price and transaction information, establishing obligations for comprehensive logging of pricing decisions, algorithm parameters, merchant fee calculations, and consumer transaction records.

**Compliance timeline.** During the interim period before April 10, 2026, regulators will organize major platform operators to conduct self-inspections against each regulatory requirement.

### PIPL Article 24 — Automated Decision-Making (eff. Nov 1, 2021)

The Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL), effective November 1, 2021, includes Article 24 as the primary provision addressing automated decision-making in pricing contexts.

**Non-discrimination requirement.** Article 24 provides that personal information handlers using personal information for automated decision-making "shall ensure the transparency of the decision making and the fairness and impartiality of the results, and may not apply unreasonable differential treatment to individuals in terms of transaction prices and other transaction conditions." The phrase "unreasonable differential treatment" in transaction prices is a direct prohibition on unjustified personalized pricing.

**Individual rights.** Where a decision that may have a significant impact on an individual's rights and interests is made through automated decision-making, the individual has the right to request clarification from the personal information handler and the right to refuse the handler making the decision solely through automated decision-making. Information push and commercial marketing based on automated decision-making must be accompanied by options not specific to personal characteristics or with convenient means to refuse.

**Relationship to Price Behavior Rules.** The December 2025 Rules explicitly reference PIPL compliance when platforms use personal data for pricing purposes. PIPL provides the rights-based infrastructure; the Price Behavior Rules provide the sector-specific prohibition.

### E-Commerce Law Article 18 (eff. Jan 1, 2019)

China's E-Commerce Law, effective January 1, 2019, was the first statutory instrument to address algorithmic personalization in the pricing context.

**Non-personalized options requirement.** Article 18 provides: "When providing the results of search for commodities or services for a consumer based on the hobby, consumption habit, or any other traits thereof, the e-commerce business shall provide the consumer with options not targeting his/her identifiable traits and respect and equally protect the lawful rights and interests of consumers." This is a disclosure-adjacent strategy: rather than prohibiting personalization, it requires platforms to offer a non-personalized baseline as an exit option.

**Penalties — Article 77.** E-commerce operators violating Article 18 face orders to correct within a stipulated period, with fines ranging from CNY 50,000 to CNY 200,000, or CNY 200,000 to CNY 500,000 in serious cases.

**Significance.** Article 18 was the earliest legislative response to dashuju shashu in China, predating PIPL by over two years. Its approach was relatively light — disclosure plus opt-out — reflecting the regulatory posture of the period. Subsequent instruments (PIPL Article 24, the Price Behavior Rules) escalated from disclosure to prohibition.

### Anti-Monopoly Law — 2022 Revision (eff. Aug 1, 2022)

The amended Anti-Monopoly Law (AML), effective August 1, 2022, was the first revision since the law's original enactment in 2008.

**Article 9 — blanket algorithmic prohibition.** Business operators shall not exploit data and algorithms, technology and capital advantage, as well as platform rules, to engage in anticompetitive conduct. This is a catch-all provision that brings algorithmic pricing within the scope of existing antitrust prohibitions.

**Dominant position abuse.** Platform operators with dominant market positions are specifically prohibited from using algorithms to analyze user preferences and transaction behavior to offer different purchase conditions or impose unreasonable limitations.

**Algorithmic collusion.** Competing undertakings shall not agree on adopting the same algorithms and platform rules to calculate prices and effectively achieve the outcome of implementing fixed prices.

**Penalty escalation.** The 2022 revision significantly increased penalties. Fines for monopolistic conduct can reach 10% of the prior year's revenue. For particularly serious violations, the revision introduced criminal liability.

### Internet Information Service Algorithmic Recommendation Management Provisions (eff. March 1, 2022)

The CAC, together with MIIT, the Ministry of Public Security, and SAMR, issued these provisions on December 31, 2021, effective March 1, 2022. This was described as the most extensive set of rules ever created for the regulation of recommendation algorithms globally.

**Algorithm registration.** Recommendation algorithms with "public opinion characteristics" and "social mobilization capabilities" must be filed with the Internet Information Service Algorithm Filing System. As of June 2024, over 1,400 algorithms developed by more than 450 companies had been filed.

**User control — Article 17.** Users must be able to view their algorithm-generated profiles and actively select and remove keywords used for recommendation. This provides a transparency and control mechanism specifically for the algorithm inputs that feed personalized pricing.

**Pricing relevance.** While primarily focused on content recommendation, the provisions establish principles of algorithmic transparency, user control, and non-discrimination that apply broadly to algorithmic systems, including those used for pricing. The algorithm filing system creates a registry that regulators can audit.

**Penalties.** Non-compliance may lead to warnings, orders for rectification, suspension of business in serious cases, and fines up to CNY 100,000.

### Anti-Unfair Competition Law — 2025 Revision (eff. Oct 15, 2025)

The revised Anti-Unfair Competition Law (AUCL), passed in June 2025 and effective October 2025, adds provisions specifically targeting algorithmic pricing in the digital economy.

**Algorithmic discrimination prohibition.** The revised law prohibits business operators from using algorithms to analyze user preferences, trading habits, and other characteristics to implement unreasonable differential treatment or other unreasonable trading conditions — a direct response to dashuju shashu.

**Platform operator obligations — Article 14.** Explicitly prohibits platform operators from forcing businesses to sell goods at prices below cost in accordance with platform pricing rules.

**Online unfair competition.** Prohibition on using data, algorithms, technology, and platform rules to engage in "online unfair competition," including forced redirects, malicious incompatibility, and other disruptive behaviors.

### Pricing Law Revision (draft July 2025, pending)

China is overhauling its Pricing Law for the first time since its enactment in 1998. A draft amendment was released for public comment in late July 2025.

**Algorithmic pricing provision.** The amendment adds a provision that operators "must not use data, algorithms, technology or rules to engage in unfair pricing behavior." This would elevate the prohibition on algorithmic price discrimination from sector-specific rules to the general statutory framework governing all pricing in the Chinese economy.

**Context.** The revision responds to destructive price competition across the Chinese economy — in early 2025, over 60 car models slashed prices, and industry-wide net margins fell to 4.3 percent. The Pricing Law overhaul addresses both algorithmic discrimination (charging different prices to different consumers) and predatory pricing (platforms forcing below-cost sales).

**Status.** Draft released for public comment. Placed on the legislative agenda of the Standing Committee of the 14th National People's Congress.

## Analysis

**Hierarchy.** Prohibition is primary. The December 2025 Price Behavior Rules represent the capstone instrument — a direct, sector-specific prohibition on algorithmic price discrimination for internet platforms, jointly issued by the three most powerful regulators in the space (NDRC, SAMR, CAC). All other instruments are supporting: PIPL Article 24 provides the rights infrastructure (individuals can refuse automated decision-making and demand explanations), the E-Commerce Law provides the disclosure baseline (non-personalized options), the Anti-Monopoly Law addresses algorithmic collusion between competitors, the Algorithm Recommendation Provisions establish the governance infrastructure (registry, transparency, user control), and the Anti-Unfair Competition Law fills gaps in the competition framework. The pending Pricing Law revision would elevate the prohibition from sector rules to the general statutory framework. The hierarchy is clear and deliberately constructed.

**Design logic.** Unified. Unlike the EU's accumulated layers or New York's differentiated sector-specific approach, China's instrument set moves in a single direction: comprehensive state prohibition of algorithmic price discrimination. Each instrument was enacted in a different year (E-Commerce Law 2019, PIPL 2021, Algorithm Provisions 2022, AML revision 2022, AUCL revision 2025, Price Behavior Rules 2025), but they form a coherent escalation sequence — from disclosure (2019) to rights (2021) to governance infrastructure (2022) to direct prohibition (2025). The temporal layering might suggest accumulated design, but the consistency of direction distinguishes it. Each instrument tightened the constraint rather than opening a new front. The December 2025 rules and the Pricing Law revision together represent the endpoint of this trajectory: an explicit, economy-wide prohibition on using personal data to set differentiated prices.

**Institution relied on — divergence.** This is the most analytically significant feature of China's profile. PIPL Article 24 is a rights-based instrument that formally confers on individuals the right to refuse automated decision-making and to demand explanations. In a rights-based strategy, the institution relied on is the individual — empowered consumers exercise their rights to discipline the market. But in China's operational reality, the institution relied on is the state. The rights exist on paper, but enforcement runs through the CAC, SAMR, and NDRC — not through private litigation or consumer exercise of data rights. The algorithm filing system makes this visible: algorithms are registered with and auditable by the state, not by individuals.

The E-Commerce Law's disclosure mechanism shows the same pattern. Article 18 requires non-personalized options to be offered, which in theory trusts the market (informed consumers choose the non-personalized option, disciplining sellers). In practice, enforcement is through SAMR administrative penalties, not through consumer market behavior. The Price Behavior Rules make this explicit: compliance is enforced through joint regulatory supervision by three agencies, self-inspections organized by regulators, and administrative penalties — not through private rights of action or market discipline.

This divergence is consistent across all instruments and constitutes a jurisdictional signature: China deploys the full range of Baldwin's strategies (prohibition, rights, disclosure, competition, code-as-regulation) but operationally trusts a single institution — the state — regardless of which strategy is formally in play.

## Evidence

**Hu v. Trip.com (2021) — first big data price discrimination case.** A VIP member surnamed Hu in Shaoxing, Zhejiang, sued Trip.com for charging her CNY 2,889 per night for a hotel room that actually cost CNY 1,377. The court ruled in her favor on both the first and second instances, ordering Trip.com to pay approximately CNY 4,800 in compensation (three times the price difference). However, the court ruled on the basis of price fraud under the Consumer Protection Act — not on the basis of price discrimination per se — because the plaintiff could not demonstrate she was treated differently from comparable consumers. The case is regarded as the first judicial recognition in China of big-data-based price discrimination, but the evidentiary difficulty in proving differential treatment (as opposed to fraud) foreshadowed the enforcement challenges that prompted legislative escalation. [Sixth Tone, "China Targets the Algorithms That Made Loyal Shoppers Pay More"](https://www.sixthtone.com/news/1018072); [Lexology, "Tighter Regulation on Personalized Pricing in China"](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=a52b5bac-66df-4a46-862e-e88002141423)

**Consumer v. Didi — SPC dismissal (2024).** A consumer filed a claim against Didi alleging differential treatment through big data and algorithms. The Beijing Intellectual Property Court dismissed the case in December 2023, and the Supreme People's Court upheld the dismissal in November 2024. The repeated failure of private litigation against algorithmic pricing is itself significant evidence: it demonstrates why China moved from rights-based instruments (which depend on individual enforcement) to direct prohibition (which relies on state enforcement). [Global Competition Review, "China: Evolving data monopoly regulation"](https://globalcompetitionreview.com/guide/data-antitrust-guide/first-edition/article/china-evolving-data-monopoly-regulation-reflects-growing-focus-anticompetitive-conduct)

**Didi — CAC data enforcement (2022).** The CAC imposed a fine of approximately CNY 8.02 billion (approximately USD 1.2 billion) on Didi Chuxing for significant violations of cybersecurity, data security, and personal information protection laws, including illegal and excessive collection of users' and drivers' personal information. While the enforcement action was framed as a data protection case rather than a pricing case, the underlying conduct — extensive collection of ride-hailing user data — is the same data infrastructure that enables algorithmic pricing. The scale of the penalty signaled the state's capacity and willingness to enforce against major platforms. [USALI, "The Didi Case and the Party's Influence over Data Enforcement"](https://usali.org/usali-perspectives-blog/the-didi-case-and-the-partys-influence-over-data-enforcement)

**JD.com — SAMR complaint (2023).** Online retailer Hangzhou Hauswirt lodged a complaint with SAMR against JD.com, alleging that JD decreased Hauswirt's product sales prices using algorithms based on data JD collected without permission, ensuring Hauswirt's price was the lowest among all livestreaming channels. SAMR accepted the complaint. This case illustrates algorithmic pricing's merchant-facing dimension — not only consumers but merchants are harmed when platforms algorithmically manipulate prices. [Gibson Dunn, "Antitrust in China — Review and Outlook 2025"](https://www.gibsondunn.com/antitrust-in-china-review-and-outlook-2025/)

**Sumscope — SAMR financial data monopoly (2024).** On September 6, 2024, SAMR issued a penalty decision against Sumscope Information Technology in its first financial data monopoly case, finding that Sumscope abused its dominant position by refusing to supply real-time transaction data to competing providers. The fine was 2 percent of 2022 annual sales revenue. While not a consumer pricing case, this demonstrates SAMR's expanding enforcement activity against data-driven market power. [Gibson Dunn, "Antitrust in China — Review and Outlook 2025"](https://www.gibsondunn.com/antitrust-in-china-review-and-outlook-2025/)

**Platform compliance announcements (2025).** Following publication of the Price Behavior Rules, several major platforms — including Dewu and Pinduoduo — announced compliance measures. Industry commentary noted that overt price double standards would "largely disappear" because such practices are easily documented with screenshots. However, platforms may shift to differentiated coupon distribution rather than direct price differences — a form of creative compliance that technically avoids the prohibition while achieving similar personalization effects. [Sixth Tone, "China Targets the Algorithms That Made Loyal Shoppers Pay More"](https://www.sixthtone.com/news/1018072)

**Algorithm registry scale.** As of June 2024, over 1,400 algorithms developed by more than 450 companies had been filed with the Internet Information Service Algorithm Filing System. The information released to the public for each filing is brief and basic — it does not reveal corporate secrets or explicate the core algorithmic power. The registry creates a compliance infrastructure and a supervisory tool for regulators, but its transparency function for consumers or researchers is limited. [Carnegie Endowment, "What China's Algorithm Registry Reveals about AI Governance" (Dec 2022)](https://carnegieendowment.org/posts/2022/12/what-chinas-algorithm-registry-reveals-about-ai-governance?lang=en)

## Movement

China's regulatory trajectory is a deliberate, accelerating escalation from disclosure to prohibition over seven years (2019–2026):

1. **January 2019**: E-Commerce Law takes effect — disclosure/opt-out (Article 18 requires non-personalized options)
2. **November 2021**: PIPL takes effect — rights-based (Article 24 prohibits "unreasonable differential treatment" in transaction prices; individual right to refuse automated decision-making)
3. **March 2022**: Algorithm Recommendation Provisions take effect — code-as-regulation (algorithm filing system, user profile control, transparency requirements)
4. **August 2022**: Amended Anti-Monopoly Law takes effect — competition (Article 9 blanket prohibition on using algorithms for anticompetitive conduct; algorithmic collusion provisions)
5. **September 2024**: SAMR Interim Provisions on Regulation of Unfair Competition on the Internet take effect — competition (enumerating digital economy unfair practices)
6. **October 2025**: Revised Anti-Unfair Competition Law takes effect — competition (explicit prohibition on algorithmic differential treatment)
7. **December 2025**: Price Behavior Rules published — prohibition (Article 15 direct ban on algorithmic price discrimination, jointly issued by NDRC/SAMR/CAC, effective April 2026)
8. **Pending**: Pricing Law revision — prohibition (would elevate ban to general statutory framework, first overhaul in 27 years)

The trajectory is one-directional: each instrument tightens the constraint. China began with the lightest available intervention (non-personalized search options in 2019) and is moving toward the heaviest (economy-wide prohibition in the Pricing Law). The escalation was driven not by a single triggering event but by a pattern of consumer complaints about dashuju shashu, failed private litigation (Hu v. Trip.com, Consumer v. Didi), and the broader policy turn toward platform regulation that accelerated from 2020 onward.

The speed of movement in 2025 is notable: three major instruments (AUCL revision, Price Behavior Rules, Pricing Law draft) within six months. This reflects a coordinated regulatory push rather than piecemeal responses.

The Cambridge Handbook of Algorithmic Price Personalization and the Law (2025, Chapter 11, by Jiangqiu Ge) notes that China's approach to algorithmic price personalization is "regulated by a dense web of legal sources and enforced by various authorities that significantly limit retailers' power to personalize prices." This characterization is accurate: the density of the instrument set is itself a feature of the regulatory design.

## Open Questions

1. **Operational enforcement of the Price Behavior Rules.** The rules take effect April 10, 2026. No enforcement actions have occurred yet. How actively will the NDRC/SAMR/CAC joint supervisory framework operate? Will enforcement be complaint-driven or proactive?
2. **Coupon migration.** Industry observers note platforms may shift from direct price discrimination to differentiated coupon distribution — technically outside the prohibition's scope while achieving similar personalization. Will regulators treat this as [[creative-compliance]] or extend the prohibition?
3. **Private litigation viability.** Both major private cases (Hu v. Trip.com, Consumer v. Didi) failed on the price discrimination claim or were dismissed. The move to state-enforced prohibition acknowledges this difficulty, but if the prohibition is enforced administratively, do consumers have any independent enforcement pathway?
4. **Algorithm registry depth.** Over 1,400 algorithms registered, but the public information is "brief and basic." Does the registry function as a genuine transparency tool, or primarily as a supervisory checklist for regulators?
5. **Pricing Law timeline.** The draft was released in July 2025, but enactment is not yet scheduled. If enacted, how would the general statutory prohibition interact with the sector-specific Price Behavior Rules?
6. **Surveillance state paradox.** China deploys pervasive state surveillance but prohibits commercial algorithmic pricing. The Cambridge Handbook (Ch. 11) notes this tension. Is the prohibition genuinely consumer-protective, or is it about consolidating data control in the state (rather than in private platforms)? This interpretive question matters for the [[surveillance-pricing-comparative-regulation|Paper 1]] topology: it determines whether China occupies the prohibition position because it values consumer autonomy or because it values state authority over data.
7. **Cross-border applicability.** The rules apply to pricing behavior "within the territory of the People's Republic of China." How will they apply to cross-border platforms serving Chinese consumers?
