# Indonesia — Surveillance Pricing Regulatory Profile

*Jurisdiction molecule for [[surveillance-pricing-comparative-regulation|Paper 1]].*

Indonesia has no instrument that directly targets surveillance pricing or algorithmic personalized pricing. Its regulatory landscape is a patchwork of general-purpose laws — consumer protection, competition, data protection, e-commerce platform governance — none of which were designed for or have been applied to personalized pricing. What distinguishes Indonesia from [[Singapore]] (its ASEAN neighbor with a similarly absent targeted regime) is not the gap itself but the nature of the gap: where Singapore's absence reflects a deliberate pro-innovation design choice backed by sophisticated voluntary governance infrastructure, Indonesia's absence reflects institutional fragmentation and legislative lag. Multiple agencies — [[KPPU]], the [[Ministry of Trade]], [[OJK]], and [[Komdigi]] — govern adjacent spaces with overlapping but uncoordinated mandates. The most distinctive feature of Indonesia's regulatory landscape is the ride-hailing tariff cap regime (Permenhub 12/2019), which represents a [[direct-action]] intervention on algorithmic pricing in transportation — not framed as surveillance pricing regulation, but functionally constraining platforms' ability to deploy unchecked dynamic pricing. The regime is in active ferment: the competition law is being amended (2026 priority legislation) with explicit provisions on algorithmic collusion and data misuse, a consumer protection law overhaul is underway, the PDP Law's automated decision-making provisions remain untested, and AI governance is being formalized through a forthcoming presidential regulation. Indonesia is a jurisdiction in transition — from absence toward framework — but as of February 2026, the framework has not arrived.

## Detail

### Consumer Protection Law (UU 8/1999) — Unfair Trade Practices (Arts. 9-17)

Indonesia's principal consumer protection statute predates the digital economy by two decades. Articles 9 through 17 prohibit a range of unfair business practices. Article 9 prohibits offering goods or services in a misleading manner regarding price, use, condition, or guarantee. Article 10 prohibits offering misleading information about prices or tariffs. Article 12 prohibits offering goods or services at special discounted prices where such offers are not truthful. Article 17 extends similar prohibitions to advertising.

**What is absent.** The law contains no provision addressing algorithmic pricing, personalized pricing, dynamic pricing, or automated decision-making. Its prohibitions target affirmatively misleading pricing representations — false discounts, deceptive advertising — not differential pricing or opaque algorithmic price-setting. A business that charges different consumers different prices for the same product based on data profiling does not violate UU 8/1999 unless it affirmatively misrepresents the price. The law's conceptual framework is pre-digital: it assumes a seller making representations to consumers, not an algorithm silently adjusting prices per user.

**Enforcement limitation.** Enforcement is diffuse. Consumer disputes are handled primarily through BPSK (Badan Penyelesaian Sengketa Konsumen), which are regional consumer dispute resolution bodies with limited capacity and inconsistent quality across Indonesia's decentralized administrative structure. Criminal penalties exist (up to 2 years imprisonment or IDR 500 million fine) but are rarely applied. BPKN (Badan Perlindungan Konsumen Nasional), the National Consumer Protection Agency, issues non-binding recommendations but lacks enforcement authority. Consumer complaints rose 84% in 2024 (1,708 complaints as of November 2024, up from 929 in 2023), with e-commerce among the top complaint sectors.

**Draft overhaul.** A new Consumer Protection Bill was circulated in 2024, proposing expanded definitions of consumers and business actors, stricter regulation of standardized contracts in digital marketplaces, enhanced consumer data protection provisions, and institutional reforms including a restructured National Consumer Protection Agency. The draft addresses digital commerce generally but does not include specific provisions on algorithmic pricing, personalized pricing, or automated decision-making in pricing.

### PDP Law (UU 27/2022) — Right to Object to Automated Decision-Making (Art. 10)

Indonesia's Personal Data Protection Law, enacted October 17, 2022 with a two-year transition period (full enforcement from October 17, 2024), is modeled on the GDPR. Article 10 grants data subjects the right to object to decision-making based solely on automated processing, including profiling, that gives rise to legal consequences or has a significant impact on the data subject. Article 1(29) defines profiling illustratively as "the activity of identifying a person with their employment history, economic condition, health, personal preferences, interests, reliability, behavior, location, or movements electronically."

**Comparison with GDPR Art. 22.** The PDP Law's Art. 10 provides a right to *object* to automated decision-making, not a prohibition or a right *not to be subject to* automated decisions. This is a weaker formulation than GDPR Art. 22, which establishes a default right not to be subject to solely automated decisions with legal or similarly significant effects, subject to specified exceptions. The PDP Law places the burden on the data subject to invoke the objection; GDPR Art. 22 places the burden on the controller to justify the automated processing. Scholars note that algorithmic pricing may not be categorized as an "automatic decision" under the PDP Law's framework, creating a gap relative to the GDPR.

**Implementation gap.** The PDP Law does not define when automated processing creates "legal consequences" or carries "significant impact." No implementing regulation has clarified whether personalized pricing constitutes a "significant impact." No guidance has been issued on how the right to object operates in practice — whether a consumer must be informed that automated decision-making is occurring (a prerequisite for exercising the right), what form the objection takes, or what remedy follows a successful objection. No enforcement action has been brought under Art. 10 for any purpose, let alone algorithmic pricing.

**Institutional uncertainty.** Enforcement of the PDP Law was assigned to the Ministry of Communication and Informatics (Kominfo), now restructured as the Ministry of Communication and Digital (Komdigi). A dedicated Data Protection Authority has not been established. The absence of a specialist DPA raises questions about enforcement capacity, particularly for technically complex algorithmic decision-making complaints.

### Competition Law (UU 5/1999) — Price Discrimination and Algorithmic Collusion

Indonesia's competition law, enacted in 1999, prohibits price fixing (Art. 5), price discrimination (Art. 6), and discriminatory practices (Art. 19(d)). Article 6 prohibits business actors from entering into agreements to set different prices for the same goods or services to different buyers. Price discrimination under Art. 6 is classified as *per se* illegal — no rule of reason analysis is required. KPPU Regulation No. 3/2011 provides interpretive guidelines for the application of Art. 19(d) on discriminatory practices.

**Algorithmic collusion gap.** UU 5/1999 was designed for an era of explicit agreements. Article 1(7) defines "agreement" as "an act of one or more business actors to bind themselves to one or more other business actors." Algorithmic collusion — where pricing algorithms converge on supra-competitive prices without explicit human agreement — does not fit this definition. KPPU has acknowledged this gap publicly: at the November 6, 2025 public hearing before Commission VI of the House of Representatives, KPPU Chair identified algorithmic collusion as a priority concern and stressed the urgency of including it in the competition law amendment. The current law's requirement of an "agreement" as a prerequisite for proving cartel behavior is inadequate for algorithmic coordination.

**Proposed amendment.** A draft amendment to UU 5/1999 is in the 2026 Priority National Legislative Program (Prolegnas Prioritas). The draft proposes to: (a) redefine "relevant market" to cover digital platforms and AI-driven transactions; (b) recognize that market dominance can arise from misuse of user data, algorithmic discrimination, or AI-based predatory pricing; (c) formally recognize economic and communication-based circumstantial evidence as legally valid "clues" (*petunjuk*) in court proceedings; (d) grant KPPU search and seizure authority for documentary and digital evidence; and (e) introduce a leniency program. As of the February 10, 2026 Parliamentary Working Committee hearing, deliberation is ongoing.

**Enforcement.** KPPU enforcement has not targeted algorithmic pricing or algorithmic collusion. KPPU's enforcement focus in 2024-2025 has been on merger review (notably the TikTok-Tokopedia acquisition, Case No. 01/KPPU-M/2025) and traditional cartel cases. In its 2021 Annual Report, only three out of 26 decisions involved discrimination cases (15% of total decisions). No decisions in 2022 or 2023 related to vertical restraints. The institutional capacity for digital market enforcement remains limited — KPPU officers were appointed as civil servants only on December 18, 2025, and KPPU currently lacks search and seizure authority.

### GR 80/2019 and MOT Regulation 31/2023 — E-Commerce Platform Governance

Government Regulation No. 80 of 2019 (GR 80/2019) on Trading Through Electronic Systems is Indonesia's principal e-commerce regulation. It establishes seven governing principles for e-commerce: good faith, prudence, transparency, trustworthiness, accountability, parity, and fair play. GR 80/2019 was amended by GR 31/2023.

MOT Regulation 31/2023 (Permendag 31/2023), issued by the Ministry of Trade and effective September 26, 2023, is the implementing regulation that imposes specific obligations on e-commerce platform operators (Penyelenggara Perdagangan Melalui Sistem Elektronik / PPMSE). The regulation's primary focus is marketplace fairness — protecting domestic micro, small, and medium enterprises (UMKM) from unfair competition by large platforms and cross-border sellers.

**Anti-price manipulation obligations.** PPMSE must implement standard operating procedures (SOPs) to supervise, avoid, and handle any form of direct or indirect anti-competitive and/or price manipulation practices. Price manipulation is defined to include price-fixing, predatory pricing, and price-gouging. Platforms must ensure that "prices of goods and/or services are free from direct or indirect manipulation practices." Platforms must report suspected price manipulation or anti-competitive behavior to KPPU within three business days.

**Platform fairness provisions.** PPMSE must provide equal business opportunities to merchants and are prohibited from interconnecting their systems with external systems in ways that facilitate data monopolization. Social commerce platforms (the provision that triggered the TikTok Shop ban and subsequent restructuring through Tokopedia) are prohibited from facilitating direct payment transactions and may only carry out promotional activities.

**What it does NOT cover.** The regulation addresses B2B marketplace fairness (platform-to-merchant relationships), not B2C personalized pricing (platform-to-consumer pricing). The anti-price manipulation provisions target platform conduct that distorts competition among sellers — predatory pricing by platforms, price-fixing among merchants — not the algorithmic individualization of consumer-facing prices based on personal data. A platform that charges different consumers different prices for the same product based on browsing behavior, location data, or device type would not violate Permendag 31/2023 unless the practice constituted "price manipulation" in the B2B competition sense. The intel assessment in [[intel-comparative-regulation]] — that this regulation "addresses e-commerce platform fairness (product display, vendor treatment) rather than consumer-facing personalized pricing" — is correct.

### Permenhub 12/2019 — Ride-Hailing Tariff Caps

The Ministry of Transportation issued Regulation No. 12 of 2019 (Permenhub 12/2019) establishing minimum and maximum tariff bands for app-based motorcycle taxis (ojek online), effective May 1, 2019. This followed years of regulatory uncertainty and public controversy over surge pricing by [[Gojek]] and [[Grab]], which together control approximately 82% of Indonesia's ride-hailing market.

**Tariff structure.** Tariffs are zoned: Zone I (Sumatra, Bali, Java excluding Greater Jakarta) IDR 1,850-2,300/km; Zone II (Greater Jakarta / Jabodetabek) IDR 2,000-2,500/km (updated to IDR 2,600/km ceiling via Permenhub 564/2022); Zone III (Kalimantan, Sulawesi, Nusa Tenggara, Maluku, Papua) IDR 2,100-2,600/km. Ride-hailing platforms are prohibited from exceeding the upper tariff limit, which directly constrains surge pricing during peak demand, traffic congestion, or adverse weather. Tariff bands are reassessed regularly but not more frequently than every six months, unless there is a sudden cost change exceeding 20% within three consecutive months.

**Significance for surveillance pricing.** This is a [[direct-action]] regulatory instrument: the state sets price bands, and platforms must operate within them. It is not framed as surveillance pricing regulation — the policy rationale is driver welfare and consumer protection from excessive fares — but its functional effect is to constrain platforms' ability to deploy unchecked algorithmic dynamic pricing. Under the tariff cap regime, a ride-hailing platform cannot use personal data, demand signals, or behavioral profiling to charge one passenger significantly more than another for the same route at the same time, because both prices must fall within the regulated band. The regime does not prohibit price differentiation within the band, and platforms retain discretion over how they allocate fares between the floor and ceiling. But it eliminates the most extreme manifestations of algorithmic surge pricing.

**Commission cap controversy (2025-2026).** A draft presidential decree would reduce the platform commission cap from 20% to 10% and require platforms to provide full accident insurance for drivers. Negotiations between the government, platforms, and driver associations were ongoing as of January 2026; the government missed its initial December 2025 deadline. The May 20, 2025 nationwide app shutdown by driver associations (Garda Indonesia and others) underscored the political salience of ride-hailing pricing and platform governance.

### OJK AI Ethics Guidelines for Fintech (Dec 2023; updated Dec 2024) and AI Governance for Banking (April 2025)

The Financial Services Authority (OJK), together with fintech industry associations, published the Code of Ethics Guidelines for Responsible and Trustworthy Artificial Intelligence in the Financial Technology Industry on December 4, 2023. The guidelines were updated in December 2024 to address generative AI, strengthen core ethical principles, and enhance provisions on consumer protection, financial inclusion, data protection, and cyber resilience.

**Core principles.** The guidelines require adherence to Pancasila (Indonesia's state philosophy); fair and accountable adherence to privacy and non-discriminatory practices; transparent and explicable application of AI (fintech operators must be able to explain how AI is used from input to output); and robustness and security. Applied to pricing, these principles would suggest that a fintech lender or insurtech provider using AI to set individualized rates should ensure non-discrimination and be able to explain the pricing logic — but as a voluntary code of ethics, not a binding rule.

**Banking AI Governance (April 2025).** OJK published the Indonesian Banking Artificial Intelligence Governance as guidelines for responsible AI use in banking, emphasizing risk assessment, transparency, and data governance.

**What neither instrument requires.** No OJK instrument prohibits algorithmic pricing discrimination in financial services, insurance, or lending. No instrument requires disclosure of personalized pricing to financial consumers. No instrument provides consumers a right to challenge AI-driven pricing decisions. The guidelines are voluntary codes of ethics, not binding regulations with enforcement teeth. The contrast with [[Singapore]]'s MAS FEAT Principles is one of institutional maturity: Singapore's voluntary framework has been operationalized through testing tools (Veritas, AI Verify) and is moving toward supervisory expectations (proposed AI Risk Management Guidelines). Indonesia's OJK guidelines remain at the ethical principles stage without comparable operationalization infrastructure.

### Draft Presidential Regulation on AI Roadmap and Ethics (Komdigi)

Indonesia's National AI Strategy (Strategi Nasional Kecerdasan Artifisial / Stranas AI 2020-2045) and accompanying AI Ethics Guidelines have existed as policy papers since 2020. In August 2025, the Ministry of Communication and Digital (Komdigi) published a roadmap and draft concept for national AI ethics guidelines, both open for public feedback. In January 2026, Komdigi confirmed that these would be formalized as Presidential Regulations, elevating the strategy and ethical principles from policy guidance into binding national instruments applicable across government, business, academia, and industry. The government had initially targeted 2025 for issuance but pushed the timeline to early 2026.

**Significance.** If finalized, the Presidential Regulation would be Indonesia's first comprehensive, cross-sector AI governance instrument with binding legal status. The strategy identifies five priority sectors (healthcare, bureaucratic reform, education, food security, smart cities) but is expected to address automated decision-making and algorithmic transparency across sectors. Sector-specific AI regulations are planned for finance, healthcare, and human resource management. Whether the final instrument will address algorithmic pricing specifically is unknown — nothing in the published drafts suggests it will.

## Analysis

**Hierarchy.** No strategy dominates. Indonesia's instrument set is dispersed across strategies — disclosure (consumer protection law, e-commerce regulation), rights and liabilities (PDP Law Art. 10), competition (UU 5/1999 Art. 6), direct action (ride-hailing tariff caps), and self-regulation (OJK AI ethics guidelines) — but none has been applied to algorithmic personalized pricing. The instruments exist in parallel, each governed by a different agency, with no coordinating hierarchy. If one were to identify a primary strategy, it would be the general-purpose competition framework (UU 5/1999), which at least contains a per se prohibition on price discrimination — but this prohibition was designed for explicit B2B agreements, not algorithmic consumer-facing pricing, and KPPU has not applied it in the digital context. The ride-hailing tariff cap is the only instrument that functionally constrains algorithmic pricing, but it is sector-specific and not framed as pricing regulation per se.

**Design logic.** Accumulated, not unified. Indonesia's instruments come from different eras with different logics: the consumer protection law (1999) reflects pre-digital trade practice concerns; the competition law (1999) reflects market liberalization-era antitrust thinking; the e-commerce regulations (2019-2023) reflect platform governance concerns driven by the TikTok Shop controversy and UMKM protection; the PDP Law (2022) reflects GDPR-influenced data protection principles; the ride-hailing tariffs (2019) reflect transportation regulation and driver welfare; the OJK AI guidelines (2023-2025) reflect global responsible AI discourse. These instruments were not designed as a coherent regulatory architecture for algorithmic pricing — they accumulated as responses to different problems at different times. The contrast with [[Singapore]] (unified voluntary governance across all sectors) and [[China]] (unified prohibition approach) is stark. Indonesia's approach most resembles the [[EU]]'s accumulated model — multiple instruments from different eras with different logics — but without the EU's institutional coordination through the European Commission.

**Institutional divergence.** The most notable divergence from theoretical expectations is in the ride-hailing tariff cap regime. Direct action — the state directly setting prices or price bands — theoretically relies on the state as the operative institution, and in this case it does. But the regime was driven not by a regulatory theory about algorithmic pricing but by a political economy of driver protests, consumer complaints about surge pricing, and competition between the traditional taxi industry and app-based platforms. The state intervened not because it diagnosed an information asymmetry or a market failure in algorithmic pricing, but because the political cost of unchecked surge pricing became unacceptable. This makes the tariff cap a politically driven direct action intervention rather than a theoretically grounded regulatory strategy — and it is the only Indonesian instrument that functionally constrains algorithmic pricing in practice.

## Evidence

**No enforcement on algorithmic pricing.** As of February 2026, no Indonesian regulator has brought any enforcement action, investigation, or inquiry specifically targeting algorithmic personalized pricing. KPPU has not applied Art. 6 (price discrimination) to algorithmic pricing. The PDP Law's Art. 10 (right to object to automated decision-making) has not been invoked in any enforcement action. The Ministry of Trade has not enforced Permendag 31/2023's anti-price manipulation provisions against personalized pricing. OJK has not taken supervisory action against algorithmic pricing in financial services.

**Ride-hailing tariff caps — operative but contested.** The tariff cap regime is the only instrument with evidence of practical impact on algorithmic pricing. Platforms operate within the regulated tariff bands, and the regime has effectively eliminated the most extreme surge pricing that characterized early ride-hailing markets. However, the regime is under political pressure from both directions: drivers demand lower commission caps (May 2025 nationwide app shutdown) while platforms resist further constraints on pricing flexibility. The ongoing negotiation over reducing the commission cap from 20% to 10% demonstrates that the regime is actively contested.

**KPPU enforcement capacity — limited.** KPPU lacks search and seizure authority under current law. Its enforcement in 2024-2025 has focused on merger review (TikTok-Tokopedia) rather than conduct cases. The institutionalization of KPPU officers as civil servants (December 2025) and the proposed competition law amendment (granting search/seizure power and recognizing digital evidence) suggest awareness of the capacity gap, but the reforms have not yet been enacted.

**Dark patterns — regulatory gap documented.** Academic research has documented that Indonesia has three significant regulatory gaps regarding dark patterns: the lack of explicit substantive prohibitions, the absence of specialized institutional oversight, and the unavailability of technical guidelines for digital interface governance. Experimental research found that drip pricing produced the highest regret and lowest fairness perception among Indonesian consumers, and low digital literacy was associated with greater susceptibility to deception. [Cambridge Core, "The Issue of Dark Patterns in Digital Platforms: The Challenge for Indonesia's Consumer Protection Law"](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/asian-journal-of-law-and-society/article/abs/issue-of-dark-patterns-in-digital-platforms-the-challenge-for-indonesias-consumer-protection-law/ED1085B81E16AB2E358AFEA91F65E251)

**PDP Law implementation — early stage.** Full enforcement of the PDP Law began October 17, 2024. No dedicated Data Protection Authority has been established. No enforcement actions under Art. 10 have been reported. Implementing regulations clarifying automated decision-making provisions have not been issued.

## Movement

Indonesia's trajectory is one of gradual accumulation with a potential inflection point approaching but not yet reached.

1. **1999**: UU 8/1999 (Consumer Protection Law) and UU 5/1999 (Competition Law) enacted — foundational statutes designed for pre-digital economy
2. **2019**: GR 80/2019 on e-commerce issued — first dedicated e-commerce regulation; Permenhub 12/2019 establishes ride-hailing tariff caps — first direct price intervention on algorithmic platforms
3. **2020**: Stranas AI 2020-2045 published as policy paper — non-binding national AI strategy
4. **2022 (Oct)**: PDP Law (UU 27/2022) enacted — Indonesia's first comprehensive data protection law, including Art. 10 right to object to automated decision-making; two-year transition period begins
5. **2022 (Dec)**: Permenhub 564/2022 updates ride-hailing tariff ceiling for Greater Jakarta
6. **2023 (Sept)**: Permendag 31/2023 issued — anti-price manipulation obligations for e-commerce platforms; social commerce restrictions (TikTok Shop response)
7. **2023 (Dec)**: OJK publishes AI Ethics Guidelines for fintech industry
8. **2024 (Oct)**: PDP Law full enforcement begins — Art. 10 automated decision-making right becomes enforceable
9. **2024 (Dec)**: OJK updates AI Ethics Guidelines to address generative AI
10. **2025 (April)**: OJK publishes AI Governance for Banking guidelines
11. **2025 (May)**: Nationwide ride-hailing driver app shutdown — political pressure intensifies on platform pricing and commission caps
12. **2025 (Aug)**: Komdigi publishes draft AI roadmap and ethics guidelines for public feedback
13. **2025 (Nov)**: Commission VI public hearing on competition law amendment — KPPU pushes for algorithmic collusion provisions; strong political support confirmed
14. **2025 (Dec)**: KPPU officers institutionalized as civil servants; competition law amendment placed in 2026 Prolegnas Prioritas
15. **2026 (Jan)**: Komdigi confirms AI roadmap and ethics will be formalized as Presidential Regulations
16. **2026 (Feb)**: Parliamentary Working Committee continues deliberation on competition law amendment

The trajectory shows a jurisdiction that is moving, but from a much lower baseline than its ASEAN neighbors. [[Singapore]] started building voluntary AI governance infrastructure in 2018 and has a coordinated multi-agency architecture. Indonesia's AI governance is still being formalized from policy papers into binding instruments. The most significant potential inflection is the competition law amendment: if enacted with the proposed algorithmic collusion and data misuse provisions, it would give KPPU institutional capacity to address digital market conduct that it currently lacks. But the amendment has been under discussion for years and its timeline remains uncertain.

The ride-hailing tariff cap trajectory is distinctive — it represents the earliest direct intervention on algorithmic platform pricing (2019), predating much of the global regulatory activity documented in other jurisdiction molecules. But this intervention was driven by transportation policy and political economy, not by a regulatory theory about surveillance pricing, and it has not been replicated in other sectors.

## Open Questions

1. **Will the competition law amendment be enacted in 2026, and will it address algorithmic pricing?** The draft includes provisions on algorithmic collusion, data misuse, and digital market dominance. If enacted, it would give KPPU search and seizure authority and the legal basis to pursue algorithmic coordination. But the amendment has been pending for years, and the 2026 Prolegnas Prioritas designation does not guarantee passage.

2. **Will implementing regulations clarify the PDP Law's automated decision-making provisions?** Art. 10's right to object to automated decision-making is unimplemented in practice. Without clarity on what constitutes "significant impact," whether personalized pricing qualifies, and how the objection mechanism works, the provision is a dead letter for surveillance pricing purposes.

3. **Will a dedicated Data Protection Authority be established?** The PDP Law assigns enforcement to Komdigi, but a specialist DPA would be better positioned to handle technically complex automated decision-making complaints. No timeline for establishment has been announced.

4. **How does the ride-hailing tariff cap regime interact with algorithmic pricing in practice?** Platforms must price within the regulated band, but retain discretion within it. Is there evidence of personalized pricing within the tariff bands — charging different passengers different amounts for the same route based on personal data, so long as both fall within the floor and ceiling? This interaction between direct-action price regulation and algorithmic pricing is theoretically interesting and empirically unexplored.

5. **Will the Consumer Protection Law overhaul address algorithmic pricing?** The 2024 draft bill addresses digital commerce generally but does not appear to include specific provisions on personalized pricing or automated decision-making in pricing. Will the final bill close this gap?

6. **Will the forthcoming Presidential Regulation on AI address pricing?** The AI roadmap identifies priority sectors (healthcare, education, food security, smart cities, bureaucratic reform) and plans sector-specific regulations. Pricing and consumer-facing algorithmic decision-making are not among the stated priorities. Will the final instrument address algorithmic pricing, or will this remain a gap?

7. **What is the practical impact of Permendag 31/2023's anti-price manipulation provisions?** The regulation requires platforms to report suspected price manipulation to KPPU within three business days. Has any platform filed such a report? Has KPPU received or acted on any reports? The provision creates a novel reporting obligation, but no evidence of its use exists.

8. **Will the proposed Grab-GoTo merger proceed, and what are its implications for algorithmic pricing?** If the merger creates a 91% market share entity in ride-hailing, the combined platform's pricing algorithm would operate in a near-monopoly environment. Would the tariff cap regime sufficiently constrain pricing power? Would KPPU impose behavioral remedies on pricing?
