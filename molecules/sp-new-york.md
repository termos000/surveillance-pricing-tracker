# New York — Surveillance Pricing Regulatory Profile


New York occupies both ends of the topology simultaneously — disclosure for general commerce and prohibition for rental housing — deploying different strategies for different sectors in the same legislative moment (November-December 2025), with early evidence of active enforcement on the disclosure side.

## Instruments

| Instrument | Strategy | Sector | Status | Enforcement |
|-----------|----------|--------|--------|-------------|
| A6765/S7033 (Nov 2025) | [[disclosure-regulation|Disclosure]] | General (insurers, GLBA-regulated financial institutions, and certain subscription pricing exempted) | Operative | NY AG; civil penalties up to $1,000/violation; cure period required before action; no private right of action |
| S7882 (Dec 2025) | [[command-and-control|Prohibition]] | Residential rental housing (rent-regulated and affordable housing programs exempted) | Enacted | NY AG (civil and criminal); private right of action under [[Donnelly Act]] with treble damages and attorneys' fees |
| S8623 (proposed Dec 2025) | Prohibition | General (would ban personalized algorithmic pricing broadly) | Proposed (referred to Rules Committee) | Would include private right of action |

## Detail

### A6765/S7033 — Algorithmic Pricing Disclosure Act (eff. Nov 10, 2025)

New York General Business Law section 349-A requires any person who knowingly advertises, promotes, labels, or publishes a personalized algorithmically-set price to a consumer in New York to include the disclosure: "THIS PRICE WAS SET BY AN ALGORITHM USING YOUR PERSONAL DATA." The disclosure must be clear, conspicuous, and easily visible — both online and in physical retail settings.

**Definitional scope.** "Personal data" is defined broadly as any information that identifies or could reasonably be linked, directly or indirectly, with a specific consumer or device. This includes location, income, browsing history, and previous shopping habits. The trigger is "dynamic pricing" (pricing that fluctuates dependent on conditions) set by an algorithm that uses personal data. One notable carve-out: location data used solely for calculating fare by mileage and trip duration does not trigger the disclosure.

**Exemptions.** Three categories are exempt: (1) insurers subject to New York insurance law, (2) financial institutions regulated under the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act or New York financial services law, and (3) subscription-based pricing that is lower than existing contract terms. The insurer exemption is a significant gap given the prominence of algorithmic risk-pricing in the insurance sector.

**Enforcement mechanism.** The NY Attorney General enforces, but must first issue a written cease-and-desist letter identifying the alleged violation and provide an opportunity to cure before pursuing penalties. Civil penalties reach $1,000 per violation. There is no private right of action under this statute, though other state consumer protection remedies remain available.

### S7882 — Algorithmic Rent Pricing Prohibition (eff. Dec 15, 2025)

New York General Business Law section 340-b amends the [[Donnelly Act]] (New York's antitrust statute) to prohibit the use of algorithmic pricing tools that perform a "coordinating function" in residential rent-setting. Governor Hochul signed the bill October 16, 2025; it took effect sixty days later.

**What it prohibits.** Two things: (1) operating or licensing software, data analytics services, or algorithmic devices that perform a coordinating function on behalf of or between two or more residential rental property owners or managers; and (2) a residential rental property owner or manager knowingly or with reckless disregard setting or adjusting rental prices, lease renewal terms, occupancy levels, or other lease terms based on recommendations from such a tool.

**Definitional scope — "coordinating function."** A tool performs a coordinating function when it does all three of: (a) collects historical or contemporaneous prices, supply levels, or lease/rental contract termination and renewal dates from two or more unaffiliated property owners or managers; (b) analyzes or processes that multi-owner information using computational methods (including machine learning and algorithms); and (c) recommends rental prices, lease renewal terms, occupancy targets, or other lease terms. The three-part conjunctive definition is critical: single-landlord systems using only their own data are not captured.

**Exemptions.** Products establishing rent or income limits for rent-regulated housing or federal/state affordable housing programs are excluded from the coordinating function definition.

**Enforcement mechanism.** Violations constitute anticompetitive conduct under the existing Donnelly Act framework. The NY Attorney General may bring civil and criminal actions. Critically, private parties (tenants) may bring civil actions, with treble damages available (three times actual damages), plus attorneys' fees. The liability standard is knowledge or reckless disregard — ignorance of a tool's data sources or processes is not a defense when this threshold is met.

### S8623 — Algorithmic Pricing Prohibition (proposed Dec 19, 2025)

S8623 would go beyond A6765's disclosure approach to prohibit personalized algorithmic pricing outright and create a private right of action. The bill was introduced December 19, 2025 and referred to the Committee on Rules.

**Significance.** If enacted, S8623 would collapse the current differentiated approach — disclosure for general commerce, prohibition for rental — into a unified prohibition strategy. This would represent a significant escalation: a first-order change in regulatory strategy rather than a refinement of the existing instruments.

**Status.** Proposed only. Referred to Rules Committee. No hearing or vote scheduled as of February 2026.

**Primary**: [S8623 text](https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/bills/2025/S8623)

## Analysis

**Hierarchy.** Neither instrument dominates. A6765 (disclosure) and S7882 (prohibition) operate in parallel, covering different sectors with different strategies. There is no explicit statutory relationship between them — they are parallel positions on the topology, not sequential rungs on a ladder. This supports the [[regulatory-topology-not-ladder]] reading: New York did not escalate from disclosure to prohibition but deployed both simultaneously.

**Design logic.** Differentiated. New York made a deliberate sector-specific choice: general commerce receives disclosure (the lightest available intervention), while residential housing receives prohibition (the heaviest). The differentiation maps onto the severity of harm and the characteristics of each market. Housing is a necessity with structural power asymmetries between landlords and tenants; general commerce involves more voluntary transactions with greater consumer optionality. The sector-specific approach also tracks institutional capacity: the Donnelly Act private right of action in S7882 relies on tenants with clear, calculable harm (the difference between algorithmically-inflated rent and competitive market rent), while A6765's AG-enforcement model reflects the difficulty of identifying and quantifying individual harm from personalized pricing in general commerce.

**Institution relied on — divergence.** A6765 is a disclosure strategy, which theoretically relies on the market — informed consumers discipline sellers. But A6765's enforcement mechanism relies on the state (AG enforcement only, no private right of action). The cure-period requirement further shifts the institution toward collaborative compliance rather than market discipline. This is a notable divergence: the strategy says "trust the market," but the enforcement design says "trust the state to nudge businesses into compliance." S7882 is a prohibition, which theoretically relies on the state. Its enforcement mechanism adds a private right of action with treble damages, which effectively recruits private actors as enforcers — a hybrid that relies on both state and private enforcement to discipline the market.

## Evidence

**First Amendment challenge survived.** In *National Retail Federation v. James*, No. 1:25-cv-05500-JSR (S.D.N.Y. Oct. 8, 2025), the court dismissed the NRF's constitutional challenge to A6765. Applying *Zauderer v. Office of Disciplinary Counsel*, the court found the mandatory disclosure "factual and uncontroversial" and reasonably related to New York's interest in preventing consumer deception. The NRF's admission that its members actually used algorithmic pricing undermined its argument that the problem was hypothetical. [Clark Hill analysis](https://www.clarkhill.com/news-events/news/ny-algorithmic-pricing-act-withstands-first-amendment-challenge/); [Troutman Pepper analysis](https://www.troutmanprivacy.com/2025/10/new-york-algorithmic-pricing-disclosure-act-upheld-as-constitutional/)

**AG enforcement activity — Instacart inquiry (Jan 2026).** On January 8, 2026, AG James sent a formal information request to Instacart following a December 2025 study by Groundwork Collaborative and Consumer Reports. The study found: (a) across 437 shoppers in four cities, nearly 75% of grocery items were offered at multiple prices simultaneously; (b) average price variance was 13% between highest and lowest prices for identical items at the same stores; (c) maximum disparity reached 23%; (d) estimated annual impact was approximately $1,200 in additional costs for a family of four. The AG alleged that Instacart's disclosures were "buried on a page only accessible by clicking on fine print text" rather than displayed prominently near prices. [AG press release (Jan 8, 2026)](https://ag.ny.gov/press-release/2026/attorney-general-james-demands-answers-instacart-about-algorithmic-pricing); [Hunton Andrews Kurth analysis](https://www.hunton.com/privacy-and-information-security-law/new-york-ag-requests-information-on-algorithmic-pricing-from-instacart)

**AG consumer alert (Nov 2025).** Before the disclosure law took effect, AG James issued a consumer alert providing examples of covered conduct (hotel rooms priced higher from affluent ZIP codes; retail prices varying between in-store and online; personalized loyalty program discounts) and encouraging consumers to file complaints. [AG press release (Nov 5, 2025)](https://ag.ny.gov/press-release/2025/attorney-general-james-warns-new-yorkers-about-algorithmic-pricing-new-law-takes)

**S7882 enforcement.** No enforcement actions or private lawsuits under S7882 found as of February 2026. The law took effect December 15, 2025 — approximately two months ago.

**Federal context — DOJ v. RealPage settlement.** The DOJ proposed settlement with RealPage (November 24, 2025) is pending court approval in the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of North Carolina. The proposed seven-year settlement limits RealPage to using only historical data at least 12 months old and prohibits granular pricing data more specific than state-wide level. Greystar separately settled for $7 million with California AG. S7882 operates in parallel to this federal action — even if the DOJ settlement is approved, New York's state-level prohibition adds an independent enforcement pathway, including the private right of action unavailable at the federal level. [DOJ press release (Nov 24, 2025)](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-requires-realpage-end-sharing-competitively-sensitive-information-and); [Wilson Sonsini analysis](https://www.wsgr.com/en/insights/doj-settles-its-algorithmic-price-fixing-case-against-realpage.html)

## Movement

New York went from zero regulatory positions to two operative instruments in approximately one month (November 10 to December 15, 2025). This is among the fastest jurisdictional movements in the global landscape. Notably, this was not incremental escalation — New York did not begin with disclosure and later add prohibition. Both strategies were adopted in the same legislative session, with A6765 part of the FY 2026 Budget and S7882 signed independently. The near-simultaneous adoption of differentiated strategies suggests a deliberate sector-specific design rather than reactive escalation.

The proposed S8623 (December 2025) signals potential further movement: from the current differentiated approach (disclosure + sector-specific prohibition) toward a unified prohibition for general commerce. If enacted, this would represent a significant shift — New York would move from trusting the market (via informed consumers) to trusting the state (via outright ban) for general commerce.

The Instacart inquiry (January 2026) is early evidence that the AG intends to enforce A6765 actively, which may influence whether disclosure proves sufficient or whether legislative pressure builds toward S8623-style prohibition.
