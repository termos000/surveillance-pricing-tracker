# US States (Composite) — Surveillance Pricing Regulatory Profile


Between 2024 and early 2026, US state legislatures produced a rapid, decentralized legislative wave targeting surveillance pricing: 51 bills across 24 states in the first seven months of 2025 alone, up from 10 bills in all of 2024, with at least 12 additional states introducing bills in January-February 2026. The wave is overwhelmingly Democratic-sponsored, concentrated in blue and purple states, and characterized by a high failure rate — as of February 2026, only one state (New York, via A3008/A6765) has enacted a surveillance pricing-specific disclosure law, while New Mexico's HB 285 (a sector-specific grocery prohibition) cleared both chambers unanimously in February 2026. The dominant strategies are prohibition and disclosure, with a notable secondary thread of sector-specific grocery/food bans. The failure rate is itself significant data: it reveals the political economy constraint on surveillance pricing regulation in the US — legislatures can propose prohibition but industry opposition (led by the [[California Chamber of Commerce]], TechNet, and the Chamber of Progress) consistently blocks enactment except where bills are narrowed to disclosure or confined to the grocery sector.

## Detail

### Strategy Type 1: General Prohibition

The largest cluster of state bills attempts outright prohibition of surveillance pricing across sectors. These bills share a common definitional core: "surveillance pricing" means offering or setting a customized price for a good or service based, in whole or in part, on personally identifiable information collected through electronic surveillance technology. The bills typically exempt loyalty programs (transparent, voluntary), broadly-defined group discounts (seniors, veterans, teachers), and price differences based on actual cost of providing the service.

**Colorado HB 1264** was the most ambitious general prohibition in 2025. Introduced February 2025, it would have prohibited using "intimate personal data" (race, weight, parenthood status, genetic information, facial geometry, political affiliations, browsing history) in automated decision systems to inform individualized prices or wages. The bill included three enforcement pathways: AG enforcement, district attorney enforcement, and a private right of action with actual damages (minimum $3,000 per violation, treble damages for bad faith). Consumer Reports, the ACLU of Colorado, and the Colorado Center on Law and Policy testified in support. Despite this, the bill was postponed indefinitely in the House Judiciary Committee on April 22, 2025.

**Georgia SB 164** replicated the prohibition-plus-wage structure, targeting both surveillance pricing and surveillance wage-setting. Enforcement would have included AG authority and a private right of action. The bill was introduced February 12, 2025 and referred to committee; it did not advance further.

**Pennsylvania HB 1942** attracted significant attention in fall 2025. Sponsored by 15 House Democrats, the bill would prohibit the use of personal data gathered by electronic surveillance technology to set customized prices, with civil penalties up to $12,500 per violation. The bill includes reasonable exemptions for cost-based price differences, publicly disclosed discount programs, and voluntary loyalty programs. Consumer Reports submitted testimony in support at the House Consumer Protection Committee hearing in November 2025. Georgetown Law's Institute for Technology Law & Policy also submitted testimony. The bill remains pending.

**Kentucky HB 33** (the "Kentucky Price Fairness Act," introduced January 2026) takes a distinctive approach: rather than creating a standalone statute, it amends the existing Kentucky Consumer Data Protection Act (KCDPA) to add surveillance pricing prohibitions. This embeds surveillance pricing regulation within the state's comprehensive privacy framework, defining "automated-decision system," "base price," "individualized data," and "surveillance pricing" within the KCDPA's existing definitional structure.

**Pattern across general prohibition bills.** The failure rate is near-total. Colorado, Georgia, and Minnesota all failed in 2025. Pennsylvania and Kentucky remain pending but face the same political economy headwinds. The most common point of failure is committee — bills die before reaching a floor vote. The exception is New Mexico HB 285, which passed both chambers unanimously, but only after being confined to grocery/retail sectors (see sector-specific discussion below).

### Strategy Type 2: Disclosure

A smaller cluster of bills requires businesses to inform consumers when algorithmically-set personalized prices are in use, without prohibiting the practice itself.

**New York A6765** (enacted; profiled in [[sp-new-york]]) is the only enacted disclosure-only instrument. Its mandatory text — "THIS PRICE WAS SET BY AN ALGORITHM USING YOUR PERSONAL DATA" — is the template that subsequent disclosure bills reference.

**Illinois HB 4248** (the "Algorithmic Pricing Transparency Act," introduced December 2025) goes beyond pure disclosure: it requires clear and conspicuous disclosure of surveillance pricing, mandates an opt-out right (consumers may opt out and receive a non-personalized baseline price), and prohibits use of specified categories of personal information for algorithmic pricing. This hybrid disclosure-plus-opt-out-plus-partial-prohibition design is distinctive and represents a middle path between pure disclosure and full prohibition.

**Illinois HB 4544** (the "Preventing Algorithmic Pricing Discrimination Act," introduced January 2026) is more narrowly a disclosure bill, tracking New York A6765's language. It exempts financial services broadly (financial institutions, broker-dealers, investment advisors, consumer credit products).

**Texas SB 2567** (introduced March 2025) would require retailers to disclose the use of AI or algorithmic pricing systems at the point of sale. The bill frames surveillance pricing as a deceptive trade practice under the Texas DTPA if undisclosed. This is the lightest-touch approach: no prohibition, no opt-out — just a transparency requirement enforced through existing consumer protection law.

**Ohio SB 79** (introduced February 2025) adds a competition-adjacent disclosure requirement: businesses with over $5 million in revenue must disclose to consumers if a "price or commercial term is set or recommended by a pricing algorithm," including whether the algorithm sets different prices for identical products and whether the business developed or distributed the algorithm. The bill's distinctive feature is its targeting of nonpublic competitor data in pricing algorithms — structurally closer to California's AB 325 (competition strategy) than to New York's A6765 (disclosure strategy), though it uses disclosure rather than prohibition as its mechanism.

### Strategy Type 3: Sector-Specific Grocery/Food Bans

A distinct wave of bills targets dynamic and surveillance pricing specifically in grocery stores and food-service establishments. This sector-specific approach has proven more politically viable than general prohibition — the only state besides New York to advance legislation to passage (New Mexico) did so through a grocery-focused bill.

**New Mexico HB 285** passed the House 67-0 and the Senate 40-0 in February 2026. It amends the Unfair Practices Act to prohibit dynamic pricing in grocery and retail stores under certain conditions: no price changes during emergencies or high-demand events, no pricing adjustments based on personal data or purchasing patterns, and mandatory conspicuous signage informing customers of dynamic pricing practices. The bipartisan, unanimous passage stands in stark contrast to the partisan failures of general prohibition bills.

**Maine HP 1055** would prohibit any "eating establishment or grocery store" from using "dynamic pricing" and require prices to be fixed for at least one business day.

**Maryland's Protection from Predatory Pricing Act** (SB 387, 2026) is a governor-priority bill from Democratic Governor Wes Moore. It would bar grocery stores from using electronic shelf labels to adjust prices in real time, prohibit using surveillance data for individualized pricing in grocery aisles, and require prices to remain fixed for at least one business day. Violations would be unfair or deceptive trade practices under the Maryland Consumer Protection Act, with civil penalties up to $10,000 for first offenses and $25,000 for subsequent violations.

**Washington HB 2481** (the "Fair Pricing and Transparency Act," 2026) combines surveillance pricing prohibition for retail goods with a four-year moratorium on electronic shelf label systems in stores larger than 15,000 square feet. The bill mandates a Department of Commerce study on ESL impact on pricing transparency and employee job security (due June 30, 2029). The committee passed the bill January 28, 2026.

**The electronic shelf label (ESL) thread.** Several grocery-focused bills target ESLs specifically — the digital price tags that enable remote, real-time price changes. Walmart's plan to deploy ESLs in 2,300 stores by 2026 has accelerated legislative concern. The ESL moratorium in Washington HB 2481 and the ESL restrictions in Maryland SB 387 represent a code-as-regulation adjacent strategy: rather than prohibiting the pricing practice, these bills target the infrastructure that enables it. At the federal level, the Stop Price Gouging in Grocery Stores Act (2026) proposed by Senators Lujan and Merkley would ban ESLs in large stores nationwide.

### Strategy Type 4: Pricing + Wages (Dual-Target Bills)

Several bills target both surveillance pricing and surveillance wage-setting — the use of automated decision systems to set individualized wages based on worker data. This dual-target approach appears in Colorado HB 1264, Georgia SB 164, Oklahoma HB 148, and at the federal level in H.R. 4640 (Stop AI Price Gouging and Wage Fixing Act). The Roosevelt Institute has been a primary intellectual driver of the pricing-plus-wages frame, arguing that the same surveillance infrastructure that enables personalized pricing enables personalized wage suppression. The dual-target bills have not fared better than pricing-only bills in terms of passage rates, but they signal an emerging analytical frame that links consumer and labor harms from the same algorithmic systems.

## Analysis

**Hierarchy.** Prohibition dominates as the proposed strategy across states, but disclosure is the only strategy that has produced an enacted, operative general-commerce instrument (New York A6765). Sector-specific prohibition (grocery) has proven more passable than general prohibition — New Mexico HB 285 passed unanimously; Washington HB 2481 cleared committee. The state-level hierarchy is thus: prohibition (most proposed, rarely enacted) > disclosure (less proposed, one enacted) > sector-specific prohibition (increasingly viable) > competition (Ohio SB 79 as sole example at state level; California AB 325 is profiled separately).

**Design logic.** Decentralized and uncoordinated. Unlike the EU's GDPR or even California's CCPA, there is no model bill or coordinating body driving state surveillance pricing legislation. Consumer Reports has provided advocacy support across multiple states (California AB 446, Colorado HB 1264, Pennsylvania HB 1942) and the Roosevelt Institute has supplied analytical framing, but the bills themselves vary substantially in definitional scope, exemptions, enforcement mechanisms, and regulatory strategy. The absence of a model bill means each state reinvents the wheel — some define "surveillance pricing" broadly (Colorado's "intimate personal data"), others narrowly (bills targeting biometric data only); some include private rights of action (Colorado, Georgia), others rely solely on AG enforcement (Minnesota, Texas); some cover wages (Colorado, Georgia), others do not.

**The political economy constraint.** The near-universal failure of general prohibition bills, compared to the success of disclosure (New York) and sector-specific grocery bans (New Mexico, and Maryland/Washington advancing), reveals a political economy pattern. Industry opposition — led by the California Chamber of Commerce, TechNet, Chamber of Progress, and National Retail Federation — is organized and effective against broad prohibition. The arguments are consistent across states: existing privacy laws are sufficient; surveillance pricing bans would stifle innovation, raise consumer prices, and eliminate beneficial personalization (loyalty programs, targeted discounts). The industry coalition gave at least $11.7 million to California legislators since 2015. This opposition is sufficient to block prohibition in committee or narrow bills until sponsors withdraw (as happened with California AB 446). However, the opposition is weaker against: (a) disclosure (New York A6765 passed, and survived a First Amendment challenge); (b) grocery-specific bans (where the necessity framing — bread, milk, produce — neutralizes the innovation/personalization argument); and (c) bills in states with less tech industry presence (New Mexico, Kentucky).

**The failure rate as data.** Of the approximately 13 surveillance pricing-specific bills introduced by July 2025, plus additional bills in late 2025 and early 2026, only one general-commerce instrument is operative (New York A6765, disclosure). Colorado, Georgia, Minnesota — all failed. California's two prohibition bills (AB 446, SB 259) — failed. The failure rate for general prohibition of surveillance pricing at the state level exceeds 90%. This is a meaningful finding for the topology: it suggests that the US political economy permits disclosure regulation and sector-specific prohibition but resists general prohibition of personalized pricing — at least at this stage of the wave.

**Relationship to federal proposals.** State bills fill a vacuum created by federal inaction. The two federal proposals — S. 232 (competition, targeting algorithmic collusion) and H.R. 4640 (prohibition of personalized pricing) — have not advanced. The Senate's One Fair Price Act (S. 3387, December 2025) is the most recent federal proposal and most closely mirrors the state prohibition bills, but faces a hostile congressional environment. State legislative activity is thus partly a response to federal gridlock: legislators are acting because Congress is not. The FTC's 6(b) study findings (January 2025) — documenting that at least 250 retailers use surveillance pricing, that intermediaries track behaviors from mouse movements to abandoned cart items — provided the evidentiary basis that state legislators cite in bill introductions.

**The 2026 acceleration.** Bills have been introduced for 2026 in at least 12 additional states: Arizona, Florida, Hawaii, Illinois, Kentucky, Maryland, Nebraska, Oklahoma, Pennsylvania, Tennessee, Vermont, Virginia, and Washington. This second wave builds on the first: Washington HB 2481 is more sophisticated than earlier bills (combining prohibition with ESL moratorium and mandated study); Maryland's governor-backed bill signals executive-branch priority; Kentucky's integration with existing privacy law (KCDPA amendment) shows regulatory learning. The wave is not slowing — it is deepening.

## Evidence

**Legislative volume.** 10 bills in 2024; 51 bills across 24 states by July 2025; bills in 12+ additional states by February 2026. This represents approximately 5x growth year-over-year and continued acceleration into 2026. [Consumer Reports Bill Tracker (2025)](https://innovation.consumerreports.org/how-u-s-states-are-tackling-algorithmic-pricing-2025-bill-tracker-and-analysis/); [MultiState Analysis (Jan 2026)](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2026/1/13/states-tackled-algorithmic-pricing-and-price-transparency-in-2025-plus-what-to-expect-this-year)

**Passage rate.** As of February 2026: one enacted general-commerce disclosure law (New York A6765); one sector-specific bill passing both chambers (New Mexico HB 285); one bill clearing committee (Washington HB 2481). All other surveillance pricing-specific bills have either failed or remain pending. The passage rate for general prohibition bills is 0%.

**Industry opposition documented.** In California, the 17 groups opposing surveillance pricing legislation gave at least $11.7 million to legislators since 2015. The California Chamber of Commerce, TechNet, and Chamber of Progress led the opposition. The National Retail Federation filed a federal lawsuit challenging New York's A6765 on First Amendment grounds — the suit was dismissed in October 2025, with the court finding the disclosure "factual and uncontroversial." [CalMatters reporting](https://calmatters.org/politics/2025/08/california-surveillance-pricing-ban/); *National Retail Federation v. James*, No. 1:25-cv-05500-JSR (S.D.N.Y. Oct. 8, 2025)

**Consumer Reports advocacy.** Consumer Reports sponsored California AB 446, supported Colorado HB 1264, supported Pennsylvania HB 1942, and submitted formal positions in multiple other states. The organization also co-authored the December 2025 Instacart pricing study (with Groundwork Collaborative) that precipitated New York AG enforcement activity. [Consumer Reports advocacy positions](https://advocacy.consumerreports.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/CR-Positions-Surveillance-pricing.pdf)

**Bipartisan grocery consensus.** New Mexico HB 285 passed 67-0 in the House and 40-0 in the Senate — unanimous or near-unanimous bipartisan support. This contrasts sharply with the partisan dynamics of general prohibition bills, which are overwhelmingly Democratic-sponsored and face unified Republican/industry opposition. The grocery sector appears to have unique bipartisan salience because of the necessity framing.

**Georgetown testimony on Pennsylvania HB 1942.** Stacey Nissenbaum (Georgetown Law's Institute for Technology Law & Policy) testified before the Pennsylvania General Assembly in November 2025, providing academic analysis of the bill's provisions. [Georgetown testimony](https://www.law.georgetown.edu/tech-institute/wp-content/uploads/sites/42/2025/11/PA-Testimony-SN.pdf)

## Movement

1. **2024 (baseline)**: 10 algorithmic pricing bills introduced across states. No surveillance pricing-specific laws enacted. FTC launches 6(b) study (July 2024).
2. **January-March 2025**: Post-FTC study, bills introduced in first wave: California (AB 446, SB 259, SB 295), Colorado (HB 1264), Georgia (SB 164), Minnesota (HF 2452/SF 3098), Ohio (SB 79), Texas (SB 2567), Massachusetts (HB 99), New Jersey (S 3612), and others. Total reaches 51 bills across 24 states by July.
3. **April-May 2025**: Colorado HB 1264 fails (postponed indefinitely in committee, April). California AB 446 passes Assembly (May). New York A3008 signed by Governor Hochul (May 9).
4. **August-September 2025**: California AB 446 gutted by Senate Appropriations Committee (narrowed to grocery only), then withdrawn by sponsor. SB 259 ordered to inactive file. Minnesota HF 2452 fails. Pattern of prohibition failure consolidated.
5. **October-November 2025**: Pennsylvania HB 1942 receives committee hearing (November). New York A6765 takes effect (November 10). New York S7882 (rental prohibition) signed and takes effect (December 15).
6. **November-December 2025**: NRF First Amendment challenge to NY A6765 dismissed (October). Illinois HB 4248 introduced (December). New York AG issues Instacart inquiry (January 2026).
7. **January-February 2026**: Second wave: bills introduced in Arizona, Florida, Hawaii, Illinois (HB 4544), Kentucky (HB 33), Maryland (SB 387), Nebraska, Oklahoma (HB 148), Tennessee (HB 1468), Vermont, Virginia (HB 121), Washington (HB 2481). New Mexico HB 285 passes both chambers unanimously (February). Washington HB 2481 clears committee (January). Maryland SB 387 introduced as governor priority. The wave accelerates rather than contracts.

**Temporal pattern.** The wave has three identifiable catalysts: (a) the FTC 6(b) study (July 2024), which provided the evidentiary foundation cited by legislators across states; (b) the New York A6765 enactment (May 2025), which provided a model for disclosure legislation; and (c) consumer anxiety around grocery prices and electronic shelf labels (late 2025-2026), which drove the sector-specific grocery ban thread. The 2025 change of federal administration and resulting federal regulatory vacuum accelerated state action — legislators explicitly cite federal inaction as motivation.
